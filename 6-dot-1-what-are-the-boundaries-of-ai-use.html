AI and Laws

Firstly, it is important to understand that there aren't actually any laws in Australia that explicitly reference AI. This is unsurprising given how new the technology is. However, that doesn't mean that there aren't any laws that apply to AI. Artificial Intelligence is a technology, and thus we'll need to look at broader, more generic laws to understanding the rules and regulation relevant to AI.

As you work through this page, keep one question in mind:

When AI creates, collects, or decides things, who owns it, who is responsible, and who is protected?

The laws that govern intellectual property in Australia are varied, depending on the type of intellectual property.

Copyright occurs by default (i.e., you don't need to apply for copyright, it is assumed) and lasts for the life of the creator plus an additional 70 years.

This sounds straightforward enough. You write a novel, you own the copyright. However, what about a novel written by AI? Does that have copyright? What about something which is a compilation of facts, rather than a strictly creative work? Is the compilation 'creative'?

Current laws in Australia (Copyright Act 1968), only applies to works which involve human authorship. Thus, 'purely AI-generated content' does not qualify for copyright protection. Human edits or selections may create copyright eligible elements to the work.

Australia also has a fair-dealing system, which places explicit limits on how copyright information can be used.

What is DABUS?

DABUS (Device for the Autonomous Bootstrapping of Unified Sentience) is an AI system created by Dr Stephen Thaler. In 2019, Thaler filed patent applications in multiple countries listing DABUS as the sole inventor of two inventions.

What happened in Australia?

In 2021, the Federal Court of Australia initially ruled that an AI could be listed as an inventor under the Patents Act 1990 — a world-first decision. However, this was overturned by the Full Federal Court in 2022, which held that an inventor must be a natural person. The High Court subsequently declined to hear a further appeal, leaving the Full Federal Court ruling as settled law.

Why does it matter?

The DABUS case is significant because it tested the boundaries of intellectual property law in the age of AI. It established that in Australia — as in most jurisdictions — IP rights require a human author or inventor. This has implications for how AI-assisted inventions are patented: the human who directs or uses the AI must be named as inventor, not the AI itself.

What does this mean for the future?

As AI systems become more capable, questions about inventorship — and authorship more broadly — will only become more pressing. Legislators in several countries are actively reviewing their IP frameworks to address AI-generated outputs. Australia's DABUS ruling provides a clear, if temporary, answer: for now, the law requires a human.

Who can use data?

Privacy Laws

The primary legal framework for protecting personal information in Australia is 1988 Privacy Act. It governs how both federal public sector agencies, and many large organisations (> 3 million turnover) are required to protect and manage personal data. There are also some greater restriction for health related information.

2024 Amendment

Amendments were made to the privacy act in 2024 which enabled individuals to pursue a cause of action for "serious invasion of privacy" to account for intrusive or misused personal data, where the breach was intentional or reckless.

It also added "doxxing" (malicious release of personal data) as a criminalised action.

How do we adjudicate harm?

While generative AI has created important questions around ownership and creativity, this isn't the only place where AI interfaces with the legal system. Most laws exist to prevent harm to people, but many make the assumption that the harm is being caused by people. This is unsurprising as the legal system functions largely by holding individuals and organisations accountable for their actions.

When AI becomes involved, this assumption is challenged. Harm can now arise from systems that operate with a degree of autonomy, without a traditional human “actor” directly causing it. This raises questions about liability, responsibility, and the scope of existing legal frameworks: who is accountable when an AI system makes a decision that results in damage, injury, or financial loss—the developer, the operator, or the AI itself? As AI increasingly participates in decision-making, the law must adapt to address harms that are indirect, emergent, or distributed across multiple human and technological agents.

Summary: Key principles from this page

  • There are no AI-specific laws in Australia — existing frameworks apply.
  • Copyright requires human authorship; purely AI-generated content is not protected.
  • The IceTV case established that copyright covers expression, not facts, and originality requires intellectual (human) choices.
  • Patent and trademark law similarly requires human inventors and applicants.
  • The Privacy Act 1988 governs how personal data is collected — including biometric and facial data used in AI systems.
  • Collection must be reasonably necessary and properly consented to — public availability is not sufficient justification.
  • Automated systems can cause large-scale harm without any individual intending it — as Robodebt demonstrates.
  • AI will increasingly challenge and ‘test’ our existing legal frameworks in the years ahead.
