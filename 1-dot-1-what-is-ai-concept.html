Artificial Intelligence: What is it?

This is an interesting question. At its core, artificial intelligence seeks to replicate human thinking. Where a machine might automate a mundane or repetitive task, AI systems seek to automate novel, complex tasks. To do so, they need to replicate skills we humans often take for granted, skills such as:

  • Reasoning
  • Decision making
  • Learning
  • Understanding speech and text
  • Identifying and categorising objects
  • And many others

So, in order to understand what artificial intelligence is, and what it can do we first need to have a basic grasp of what it is trying to replicate: human thought.

Artificial intelligence is a very old discipline, beginning the 1950's inspired by research into squid brains (Figure 1, which gave the first indication of how actual brains work). When we discovered the basic building blocks of brains (neurons), the idea of building an artificial brain became plausible.

In fact, researchers at the time were... let's say "ambitious". There are many contemporary quotes from the 1960's indicating how confident researchers were to replicate human brains.

Within a generation, I am convinced, few compartments of intellect will remain outside the machine’s realm—the problems of creating “artificial intelligence” will be substantially solved.

Marvin Minsky, 1967

 

Machines will be capable, within twenty years, of doing any work a man can do.

Herbert Simon, 1965

 

That said, not everyone was so confident (Figure 3).

The problem is, that brains are much more complicated than tying a few neurons together (even single brain cells are incredibly complex). Undeterred, researchers began developing algorithms and processes which tried to emulate human thinking, mostly starting with formal logic. The field continued to innovate over the next half a century with many problems being solved, and others proving very elusive, leading us to the modern day.

Artificial Intelligence: Large Language Models

It is unlikely that your interactions with the term 'artificial intelligence' has had much to do with squid axons or even formal logic. Most of AI up until this point was limited to dedicated tasks, such as object recognition, translating between languages or winning games of chess. In November 2022, OpenAI (a non-for-profit organisation) released their new tool: ChatGPT 3.5, a large language model, and a chatbot. This was the first time in history, that AI really lived up to its name. The promises made in the 1960's seemed to have finally come true.

ChatGPT provided a prompt-based chatbot. Unlike previous chatbots, which were easily tricked, had poor memories and were easy to spot, ChatGPT (and its cousins like Grok, Bard, Gemini etc.) were able to conduct complex conversations, apply abstract reasoning and very much give the impression of being 'intelligent' programs. Moreover, these programs have subsequently been hooked into other systems, allowing them to create and interpret images, talk like a person, translate languages and do a whole host of seemingly amazing tasks. One problem we have now, is that the term artificial intelligence really has two meanings. One is the field of research which contains all manner of different subfields. The second is the investment buzzword of the 2020's. To give you a proper understanding of what the difference is, here is a diagram to show you AI, its subfields (Figure 3). You'll note that artificial intelligence appears twice, once as the field in general, and once as a sub-sub-sub-sub-field. Confusing right?

Artificial Intelligence: What is this course about?

So, let's be honest. The reason this course exists is because of the small blue bubble (Figure 1). The development of large language models has had a huge impact on society. Whole sectors of the economy are re-orienting themselves as AI supplants work that used to be the exclusive domain of humans. However, this course isn't going to just look at the 'latest new technology'. We're going to investigate all kinds of AIs, from the ubiquitous large language models (ChatGPT, Gemini etc.) to other kinds of generative tools (Dall-E, Midjourney etc.) to embedded AI tools (like Grammarly, social media, autocomplete etc.) and even the kinds of AI which influence politics and business decisions.

AIs are now, literally everywhere. If you use search engines, map software, social media, or the internet in general there is an AI paying attention to everything you do. When you drive down the expressway, there is an AI trying to spot you using your phone. When you buy... anything, there are AIs keeping track of your purchasing habits whether it is through your bank, or through loyalty programs. In this course, we are going to consider what having AI means for people, what impacts it is having on people and society and most importantly... should we be using it at all?
