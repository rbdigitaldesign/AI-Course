Creating Your AI Toolkit

This page will help you explore a range of AI tools that can support learning and research. You will try a few platforms, experiment with prompts, and reflect on how these tools can be used ethically and effectively in academic work. We will prioritise tools you may be less familiar with, rather than only using common options such as ChatGPT or Grammarly.

Step 1: Should You Use AI In The First Place?

Best practice starts with a decision. Before you open a tool, check whether AI use is appropriate for the task you are doing. This course is not about using AI at every opportunity. It is about knowing when to use it, when not to, and how to justify your choice.

Quick Decision Check

Ask Yourself:

  • What are the rules? Is there specific guidance in my course about what I am/am not allowed to do?
  • What is the task? Am I generating ideas, improving clarity, exploring sources, or producing assessable work?
  • What is the risk? Could AI introduce misinformation, fabricated citations, bias, or oversimplification?
  • What is required of me? Is original analysis, interpretation, or evidence of my thinking required here?
  • Can I verify? Do I have time and access to check claims and sources independently?
  • Will I disclose? If I use AI, how will I describe what I used it for, and what I verified myself?

Rule of thumb: If AI would replace your reasoning or evidence, stop. If AI supports your thinking and you can verify outcomes, proceed.

Common Appropriate Uses In Study And Research

  • Brainstorming angles or research questions
  • Creating a draft structure you then rewrite
  • Generating search terms and identifying concepts to investigate
  • Checking clarity and grammar (without changing your meaning)
  • Summarising your own notes or a transcript you provide, then validating key points
  • Providing critique on drafts and final work (as long as you ask only for critique and not the fixes).

Common High-Risk Uses

  • Submitting AI-written text as your own work
  • Accepting citations without checking the original sources
  • Using AI to make claims that require evidence, without verification
  • Using AI to complete tasks intended to demonstrate your learning process
  • Using AI outputs as “proof” in areas where accuracy is critical (health, law, safety, finance) without expert confirmation

Step 2: If The Answer Is “Yes”, Use Prompt Engineering To Improve Quality

If you decide AI use is appropriate, prompt engineering is a practical way to improve clarity, relevance, and usefulness. It also helps you be more transparent about what you asked for and why.

Step 3: Tool Trials (Build Your Toolkit)

Choose Two tools below to trial. For each tool, start with the sample prompt, then improve it using the prompting skills from the interactive above. Your goal is not to “get an answer”, it is to evaluate whether the tool is appropriate for academic work and how you would use it responsibly.

Tool 1: Gemini (Google), Deep Research Assistant

Purpose: Supports literature reviews, background research, and comparing sources and methods.

Try This:

  1. Choose a topic from a current or past course.
  2. Run the sample prompt once as written.
  3. Rewrite the prompt using the prompt engineering interactive.
  4. Compare the two outputs.

Evaluate:

  • Are the sources credible and relevant?
  • Does it cite specific papers, or does it generalise?
  • What would you verify using Google Scholar or library databases?

Sample Prompt: “Summarise recent peer-reviewed research on [Insert Topic]. Include key findings, methodologies, and gaps in the literature.”

Note: In Gemini, you may need to enable “Deep Research” (if available) before running this task.

Tool 2: Perplexity, Research And Source Discovery

Purpose: Supports source discovery and comparing viewpoints, often with citations included.

Try This:

  1. Choose a topic you are considering for an assignment or presentation.
  2. Run the sample prompt once as written.
  3. Improve the prompt using the prompt engineering interactive, including source and date constraints.
  4. Compare outputs and citation quality.

Evaluate:

  • Are the definitions accurate and specific?
  • Are the citations real and traceable?
  • Are viewpoints meaningfully different, or just lightly varied?

Sample Prompt: “What are the main ethical concerns in using AI for student assessment in higher education? Provide definitions of key concepts and contrasting viewpoints.”

Tool 3: Podwise Or Podcast.ai, Audio Summarisation

Purpose: Supports summarising long-form audio (lectures, podcasts, interviews) to help review and extract themes.

Try This:

  1. Choose a course-relevant lecture recording or podcast episode.
  2. Run the sample prompt once as written.
  3. Improve the prompt (add purpose, audience, and output format) using the prompt engineering interactive.
  4. Compare summaries for accuracy and completeness.

Evaluate:

  • Did it capture the main argument and key evidence?
  • What was missed or oversimplified?
  • What would you still need to listen to directly?

Sample Prompt: “Summarise the key points from [Insert Link And Title]. Highlight ethical guidelines and practical recommendations.”

Tool 4: Research Mapping And Synthesis (Choose One)

Pick One Tool and trial it using the same process (run, improve prompt, compare, evaluate).

  • ResearchRabbit (Visualising literature and networks): https://www.researchrabbit.ai/
  • Consensus (Evidence-led answers from papers): https://consensus.app/
  • Elicit (Streamlining research and extracting findings): https://elicit.com/
  • Scite (Smart citations and how papers are cited): https://scite.ai

Evaluation Focus: Does the tool help you verify claims and sources, or does it mainly generate fluent summaries?

Tool 5: Writing And Feedback Support (Choose One)

Pick One Tool and trial it on a short piece of your own writing (or a draft paragraph you create for this activity).

  • Paperpal (Academic writing support): https://paperpal.com/home
  • Thesify (Editing and feedback): https://app.thesify.ai/

Evaluation Focus:

  • Did the tool improve clarity without changing your meaning?
  • Did it introduce new claims you did not make?
  • What would require disclosure in an assessment context?

Reflection Questions

  • Which tool did you find most useful for your learning or research, and why?
  • To what extent did the AI support your thinking, or replace your thinking?
  • What limitations or risks did you notice (accuracy, bias, missing sources, oversimplification)?
  • What would you verify independently before relying on the output?
  • How would you disclose your AI use in a future assessment?

How AI Can Support Different Roles In Education (Quick Scan)

AI can support students, educators, and administrators, but best practice depends on context, risk, and transparency.

  • Educators: Course design support, feedback drafting, marking consistency checks
  • Students: Brainstorming, study planning, clarity and grammar, feedback rehearsal
  • Administration: Summarising policies and procedures, drafting communications (with review)
