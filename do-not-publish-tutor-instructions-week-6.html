Tutor Instructions

Overview

The main objectives of this workshop are:

#1: Challenging students to think about what is and what should be when it comes to AI regulation.

  • Exposing students to different international perspectives.
  • Decomposing some of the legal implications of AI.

#2: Reinforcing the difference between Legal and Ethical Frameworks.

  • The Manifesto task challenges students to align their AI use with their more general beliefs. For example, is using AI theft?
  • This may require students to consider concepts like agency and whether this applies to Artificial Systems, especially around decision making.

Task #1: Case Study Discussion (30 minutes)

This task is a sit and discuss task. If the students are poorly prepared, they can use AI to summarise the articles in question (then perhaps challenge them on the accuracy of the summary).

The goal is to evoke ideas around the concept of ownership, theft, originality and privacy. Try to encourage students (table-by-table) to challenge the assumptions behind the laws that apply to their case studies. What are the laws assuming etc etc.

Logistically, break this down into "Individual Activity/Table Discussion/Class Discussion" (10 minutes/10 minutes/10 minutes).

Task #2: The Good, the Bad and the Ugly Revisited (30 minutes)

This task is similar to the first. Students are being asked to actively review their previous week's work (which will form part of the ePortfolio). This should be used as an opportunity to describe the ePortfolio as a 'living document' (rather than a one and done thing).

The goal here is to get students to the idea that writing laws around AI will be difficult due to the stakeholders involved, but probably necessary in some sense (to avoid dystopian nightmares or promote utopian paradises).

Logistically, break this down into "Individual Activity/Table Discussion/Class Discussion" (10 minutes/10 minutes/10 minutes).

Break

Task #3: AI Manifesto (50 minutes)

This task requires students to lay what they actually believe on the table. It is difficult enough to come up with a regular manifesto. For struggling students, you can suggest they simply pick a word such as a religious background or ethical framework to explore using an LLM if they are struggling to present their own perspective ("I am a zealous libertarian" etc.).

Once students have produced their own manifesto, have them discuss it at the table.

Finally, you could create a 'ten commandments' task. Have students suggest a number of 'Thou shalt not...' and have an ad-hoc voting game in class to see what the common themes of the manifestos are.
