Artificial Intelligence (Traditional)

The reality is that traditional Artificial Intelligence techniques (those not utilising Machine Learning) are slowly dying.

Search Algorithms

Search Algorithms

Search algorithms are still used today in many small-scale computer science applications. These algorithms deal with the issue of finding the best path. For a simple example, imagine wanting to fly between two cities within Europe. There are probably dozens of different ways to fly with all manner of connecting flights. Search algorithms are used to optimise these decisions. Any problem that can be converted into a set of nodes (locations) with lines between them (Figure 1) is suitable for search algorithms (as long as the graph is not too big).

Figure 1: Simple representation of a path in a computational graph.

Famously, search algorithms were used to defeat Gary Kasparov at chess (DeepBlue).

Expert Systems

Expert Systems

Expert Systems were an early attempt to encode logical reasoning into a machine. If you can encode all the things you know to be true and all the causative statements that are logical, an Expert System can use an Inference Engine to infer new rules and determine the truth of new statements. They were used historically in areas such as medical diagnosis and finance, but have since been replaced with Machine Learning.

Expert Systems illustrate one of the flaws in thinking of early AI development. A lot of human thought isn't entirely deterministic, nor is it completely logical. However, getting a good intuition (i.e., some randomness) can be as helpful (or more helpful) than following strict logic.

Medicine

Diagnosis

AI has numerous applications within medicine, specifically in the area of diagnosis and disease detection. ML-based models have been shown to out-perform humans at numerous tasks such as analysing X-rays (Figure 2), radiographs, MRIs and all manner of imaging techniques. 

Figure 2: Lung Cancer detection outperforms humans (source)

Testing

ML-based models can also predict risk factors for various diseases by analysing demographics, habits, blood markers and all manner of measurements (review article). Why do an expensive laboratory test when you can infer the result from existing data using an ML model. These kinds of small efficiencies allow the medical system to run more cheaply by prioritising testing for different patients depending on their risk profiles.

Policy

Going forward, governments will need consider how best to integrate these emerging technologies into our healthcare system.

Mining Attention

Attention

One of the big uses of AIML is social media. On any website which has a 'feed' (an infinite number of things to show you), AI is used to maximise engagement. The longer you are on the platform, the more advertisements you can be shown and the more money can be made. This could be photos of your friends, jokes/memes or shortform video content. Recommendations

Recommendation

If you use online shopping, streaming services or really any website, there is limited real-estate. There are only so many products, TV-shows or movies that can fit on a page. As the currency of such businesses is attention, working out which product to display or which video to recommend is big business. More clicks equals more revenue, so knowing what you like is key to maximising value. Machine Learning models perform better the more information they have, so knowing your demographics, previous purchasing history and even your social network will influence what websites will show to you. These systems often go further, personalising what you see to match your aesthetic preferences (Figure 4).

Large Language Models

Customer Service

AI Chatbots

Many websites now employ AI chatbots. These LLM-backed systems can take user questions and handle simple inquiries. Depending on the industry and organisation, how these chatbots handle complex queries varies.

For example, Lyft (a car sharing service), currently use Claude AI to answer routine customer service questions such as account access, requirements, cancellations and general FAQs (article). Upon detecting a complex issue, such as safety, it automatically directs the conversation to a human support agent.

AI Support Agents

Unlike chatbots, some Agents aren't exposed directly to the customer. Instead, these 'internal workers' handle customer inquiries, but leave final deliberation to a human.

For example, Salesforce's AI Agent 'AgentForce', uses LLMs to handle customer inquiries including drafting responses based on customer history and similar cases (article). This has allowed the organisation to dramatically reduce its staffing numbers (from 9000 to 5000).

Computer Programming

Software Engineering

AI is being used across the entire software engineering lifecycle. LLMs can be used to interpret user requirements from vague text, suggest design strategies based on existing code-bases, update documentation, help with automated code generation, generate tests and better manage deployment and maintenance tasks.

Despite these gains, there is still a need for human oversight. LLMs in software engineering are augmenting existing coders, but not truly replacing humans entirely.

Data Summarisation

Data Labelling

Every day, millions of articles are being written on all kinds of topics (many by LLMs). If you are in finance or commerce, it is helpful to know 'what is going on'. Unfortunately, no one has time to read everything, but LLMs can be used to massively reduce this workload. LLMs are used to summarise documents in real time, such as labelling news articles based on their key topics, locations etc. These curated lists can then be sold to other interested organisations who can then make informed business decisions.

Data Summary

Similar to data labelling, LLMs are being used to take large articles and distill them down into a few major key points. This is a similar technology to NotebookLLM which creates podcasts of documents.

Search Engines

Search Engines

One of the more obvious uses of LLMs is for search engines. Traditionally, these systems would crawl across the internet counting how popular different websites were (usually by counting how many other websites link to them). Now, asking any question of a search engine will likely result in an 'AI answer' first and foremost, followed by old set of website links (usually paid links, then eventually non-paid links).

In general, prior to LLMs, a lot of what people used the internet for was to 'ask questions' rather than 'find things'. This resulted in web-pages like Wikipedia being at the top of many web-searches. While Wikipedia has always been troublesome for academia (it is not considered a reliable source), the information presented in 'generally true'. What is helpful about websites like Wikipedia is that you can audit the articles and cross reference them against actual sources. Hence, you can make claims like 'most of what is written on Wikipedia is factually accurate'. However, unlike static websites, search engine 'AI answers' are largely stochastic (i.e. a bit random) so it is less clear how good these answers actually are and it is harder to measure.

AI Content Generation

AI Content Generation (i.e., Slop)

Many websites employed articles written by Artificial Intelligence prior to the boom of LLMs in 2022. However, now AI-generated content can be found all over the internet. For example in 2024, Cosmos Magazine (an offshoot of CSIRO) began using AI to generate articles. However, due to public backlash, this ended shortly after. Interestingly, CSIRO discontinued Cosmos in 2025 a hint of how AI-generated content may be having an impact on an already stretched sector.
