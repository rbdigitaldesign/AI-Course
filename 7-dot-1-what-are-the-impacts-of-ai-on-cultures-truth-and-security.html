AI as Subject, Tool and Creator

Artificial Intelligence is often described as a technological revolution, but it’s also a cultural one. Even before AI became a tool to create art, it has captured the public imagination. AI in fiction (i.e., inspiring art) goes as far back as 1927's Metropolis (Figure 1) and continues to this day. This media has to a large extent, shaped the way people think about AI (for better or worse). In this sense, AI is the subject of artistic expression.

AI has also been used to explicitly create cultural artefacts. In fact, for some art forms, it is virtually impossible to reproduce them without Artificial Intelligence, which is central to the artistic expression. Examples of this kind of art include robotic sculptures whose artistic value is expressed in their behaviour, as well as their appearance (Figure 2 - left). AI also forms an integral part of modern video-games, where characters, enemies, behaviours and physics often rely on AI for various purposes. AI can be used as a human-like competitor (think DeepBlue and AlphaGo), but AI has been used for far more visceral storytelling. Characters and entities empowered through AI can achieve life-like or pseudo-lifelike behaviour designed to evoke emotional responses in the player. Examples include games such as Ico and Shadow of the Colossus (Figure 2 - right), where AI is used to bring life to these artificial creations. In this view, AI can be seen as a tool and medium for artistic expression.

There were also early attempts at placing AI as the creator of art. Traditionally, the creation of art has required many different components (concept, composition, style, execution) and attempts have been made to create visual and audio art through Artificial Intelligence and procedural generation (Figure 1). These AI-backed tools used more explicit rules, trying to synthesise the 'essential' characteristics of different parts of art, then allowing AI to curate the content using randomness and rules to create art. For examples see (Context Free Art). In this view, AI can be seen as a creator (or co-creator) of artistic expression.

AI as a Technological and Creative Revolution

As AI systems become more capable of generating images, music, video, and text, they are reshaping how we create, share, and understand meaning. AI doesn’t just automate tasks; it automates creativity, communication, and even deception. It can preserve endangered languages, generate stunning artworks, and help us imagine new futures. But it can also produce deepfakes, spread misinformation, and challenge our ability to trust what we see and hear. So, how can we balance the possibilities with the problems?

On the one hand, AI can act as a preserver of culture. It can help reconstruct lost artworks, translate or even revive endangered languages, and provide new ways of accessing and engaging with heritage. It can enable creators to experiment with form and imagine entirely new aesthetic horizons. On the other hand, these same technologies can disrupt culture in ways that are harder to celebrate. They blur the lines between human and machine authorship, often without crediting the original sources that fuel them. They can reproduce biases, distort traditions, or flood creative spaces with machine-generated work that risks overshadowing human voices. In short, AI is not just a tool for efficiency; it is actively shaping culture itself, for better and for worse. These examples illustrate that the cultural revolution brought by AI isn’t abstract, it involves legal, ethical, and economic consequences, especially for artists, writers, and creators whose work is being used in ways they may not have anticipated or agreed to.

Creativity and the Question of Ownership

Perhaps nowhere is the cultural impact of AI more visible than in the creative industries. Tools like Midjourney, DALL·E, and Suno can now produce strikingly convincing images, music, and video in a matter of seconds. What once took hours of human labour and emotional investment can be replicated almost instantly, and often with remarkable polish. But this raises difficult questions. Who owns AI-generated art? Traditionally, creativity has been viewed as deeply human, rooted in experience, memory, and emotion. By contrast, AI doesn’t “feel” or “reflect.” It recombines data (massive amounts of it) scraped from the internet, usually without consent or compensation. The results may look original, but they are built on the work of countless unnamed creators. This tension has led to both outrage and opportunity. Many artists argue that AI art amounts to theft, appropriating style and substance without recognition. At the same time, others see AI as a new medium, a collaborator that can expand creative possibilities. The legal system is still catching up. Disney and NBC have sued Midjourney; Getty Images is pursuing Stability AI; and collectives of artists are challenging Stable Diffusion. In response, many AI platforms have begun limiting prompts that directly copy existing styles, but the underlying issue remains unresolved. As some examples:

  • Warner Bros Discovery has recently sued Midjourney for allegedly using its famous characters, including Superman, Batman, Wonder Woman, Bugs Bunny, and Scooby-Doo, to train image generation models. The complaint claims that by removing safeguards, Midjourney allowed users to create and download images of these characters in new scenes without authorisation.
  • Meanwhile, in a case against Anthropic, authors alleged the company used pirated books to train its AI systems. In 2025, Anthropic agreed to a $1.5 billion settlement. The case raises major questions about how these systems are fed, how training data is acquired, and whether copyrighted works can be used without compensation.
  • On the research front, there are concerning findings about large language models: some have memorised large portions of copyrighted books. One study showed that certain open-weight models (including Llama 3.1 70B) memorised nearly entire texts of books like Harry Potter and 1984. That has implications for infringement, originality, and the boundary between training and copying.

And beyond the legal lies the philosophical: if AI can compose a symphony, paint in the style of a master, or write poetry in seconds, what becomes of the value of human creativity? Is originality still meaningful? Is intention what makes art, or is the artefact itself enough? These are not easy questions, but they go to the heart of what culture means in the age of AI.

For you as students: ownership isn’t about just who pressed “generate.” It’s about whose work was used, how, and who is benefiting, or being harmed, in the process.

Truth in the Age of Synthetic Media

One of the most unsettling abilities of AI is its power to generate synthetic media, content that looks and sounds real, but isn’t. Deepfakes can place someone’s face and voice into fabricated videos. AI-written articles can mimic journalistic style. Entire social media identities can be automated and maintained by bots.

This fundamentally destabilises how we judge what is true. In the past, we could rely on trusted institutions, corroboration, or the apparent authenticity of photographs and videos. Today, even the most convincing evidence might be manufactured. A political speech could be fabricated. A viral news story could be written entirely by an algorithm. Even the familiar faces of friends or leaders can be manipulated with unnerving realism.

Synthetic media has moved from sci-fi speculation into everyday risk. Trust is under pressure. Reports of deepfake incidents are increasing. Tools for detecting deepfakes are increasingly being deceived. This is having real-world imapcts. For example, deepfake vishing (voice phishing) has surged. In Q2 2025, deepfake vishing attacks rose by ~170%. Scammers are cloning voices of executives or leaders to trick employees into urgent wire transfers. In one case, a European energy company lost US$25 million because someone impersonated the CFO via a cloned voice and got an order signed. (Langly, 2025)

The implications are profound. Misinformation spreads faster and with more impact than ever before. Trust in media and institutions erodes. Public opinion can be manipulated with unprecedented ease. Personal reputations can be destroyed.

Yet synthetic media isn’t only dangerous. It can be used in satire, creative expression, or even education, re-enacting historical figures, for example, in ways that make learning more vivid. The problem is not the existence of synthetic media itself, but the difficulty of distinguishing between its responsible and harmful uses.

These developments make truth slippery. The question isn’t “can we create convincing fake content?” but “can we reliably spot it, and hold people accountable when it’s used to harm?”

AI and Cultural Preservation: A Double-Edged Sword

Despite these risks, AI also holds promise for cultural preservation. In some communities, AI is being used to revitalise endangered languages, document oral histories, and support Indigenous knowledge systems.

For example, machine learning tools have been used to assist with speech recognition and text generation for languages with very small numbers of fluent speakers, enabling language learners to practice pronunciation and vocabulary at scale. In Australia, AI-supported tools have been explored for the documentation of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander languages, many of which are critically endangered.

AI can also assist in preserving cultural artefacts by reconstructing damaged artworks, restoring historical audio recordings, or digitising fragile manuscripts and artworks for future generations (ref). In museums and archives, AI is increasingly used to catalogue collections, identify patterns across large cultural datasets, and make heritage materials more accessible to the public.

However, this promise comes with serious ethical risks. Cultural knowledge—particularly Indigenous knowledge—is not simply data. It is often collective, contextual, and governed by cultural protocols about who may access, share, or interpret it. When AI systems are trained on cultural materials without proper consent or governance, they risk extracting knowledge in ways that replicate colonial dynamics: taking, abstracting, and repackaging culture for external benefit.

There is also the danger of cultural flattening. AI systems tend to generalise and standardise patterns. When applied to cultural heritage, this can erase nuance, sacred distinctions, or local meaning. For example, an AI model trained on Indigenous art styles may reproduce visual motifs without understanding their ceremonial, geographic, or spiritual significance, potentially leading to misrepresentation or misuse.

For these reasons, many scholars and Indigenous organisations argue that AI systems engaging with cultural heritage must follow principles of Indigenous Data Sovereignty. These principles emphasise community authority over data, collective consent, and the right of Indigenous peoples to control how their cultural knowledge is represented, stored, and used. In this context, ethical AI is not just about avoiding harm—it is about respecting cultural authority and ensuring that technology supports cultural continuity rather than exploitation. AI can support cultural preservation, but only when communities are actively involved in governance, design, and decision-making. Without this, preservation risks becoming another form of extraction.

Suggested references

  • Kukutai, T., & Taylor, J. (2016). Indigenous Data Sovereignty: Toward an Agenda. ANU Press.

  • United Nations (2018). Principles for Indigenous Data Governance.

  • Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies (AIATSIS). Code of Ethics for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Research.

But even here, caution is needed. AI systems are shaped by the data they are trained on and by the values of those who build them. Without careful design and community involvement, AI tools risk flattening cultural nuance or reinforcing dominant narratives.

Security, Privacy, and the Need for Governance

Beyond culture and truth, AI raises urgent concerns about security and privacy. These systems rely on enormous amounts of data, much of it personal. They are often opaque, operating in ways that even their creators do not fully understand. And they can be hacked, manipulated, or misused.

Every time you interact with an AI, through a chatbot, a recommendation engine, or facial recognition, you are sharing data. That data can improve services, but it can also be exploited, leaked, or sold. Moreover, AI can reinforce bias, enable surveillance, and be deployed for fraud or identity theft.

This is why governance matters. Laws and policies are struggling to keep pace with the speed of technological change, but without oversight, AI risks undermining security, privacy, and democratic accountability. Companies must take responsibility for their systems. Governments must craft effective regulations. And individuals must remain aware of the risks, taking steps to protect their own digital identities.

Recently exposed security and privacy vulnerabilities in AI include:

  • In Australia, staff of major institutions—including banks like the Commonwealth Bank have raised concerns that employer monitoring through AI tools (location data, keystrokes, email monitoring) is being done with little oversight or clarity on how the data is used.

  • Recently exposed security and privacy vulnerabilities in AI include:

    • Workplace surveillance in Australia: Reports from unions and media investigations have highlighted the growing use of AI-driven employee monitoring tools in Australian workplaces. These systems can track keystrokes, screen activity, location data, and communication patterns, often without transparent disclosure of how data is stored, analysed, or retained. Critics argue that such tools risk normalising constant surveillance and undermining worker autonomy and trust.

    • Data leakage through AI tools: Multiple incidents have shown that employees unintentionally upload sensitive corporate or personal information into generative AI systems, where it may be stored or used for further training. This has led several governments and companies to restrict or ban the use of public AI tools for confidential work.

    • Model inversion and data extraction attacks: Researchers have demonstrated that it is sometimes possible to extract personal or sensitive information from trained AI models, particularly large language models, raising concerns about whether data used during training can ever be fully anonymised.

    • Facial recognition and mass surveillance: AI-powered facial recognition systems have been deployed by governments and private companies worldwide, often without clear legal frameworks. These systems disproportionately misidentify people from marginalised groups and raise serious concerns about civil liberties, consent, and the normalisation of surveillance.

    Together, these examples show that AI security risks are not hypothetical. They affect workplaces, governments, and everyday citizens. Governance is therefore not optional; it is essential for maintaining privacy, trust, and democratic accountability.

But governance is not only about regulation. It is also about values. What kind of AI do we want to build? Who gets to decide how it is used? How do we balance innovation with accountability? These questions are not abstract—they shape the everyday systems we interact with, from social media feeds to border security.

As you reflect on this topic, consider your own role. How do you protect your digital footprint? What risks do you take when you use AI tools? And what kind of future governance structures would you want to support to ensure AI serves the public good rather than narrow interests?

Governance is often framed as a technical or legal challenge, but it is also a cultural one. Decisions about how AI is regulated reflect values about autonomy, creativity, fairness, and trust. Different societies will make different choices, and those choices will shape how AI is woven into everyday life. Effective governance does not mean stopping innovation. It means setting boundaries that protect human dignity, cultural diversity, and public trust while allowing beneficial uses to flourish. Universities, as sites of knowledge production and cultural influence, play a critical role in modelling responsible AI use—through research, teaching, and institutional practice. As students, you are not passive observers of this process. Your choices—what tools you use, what data you share, how critically you evaluate AI-generated content—contribute to shaping the norms of the AI-driven world you are entering.
