Different countries are taking different approaches to AI...

Copyright in the United States

Overseas, the topic of ownership is still an open question. Consider the case study below:

[NYT vs OpenAI Case Study – H5P]
[Page 1]

In December 2023, the newspaper publisher The New York Times Company filed a lawsuit against OpenAI and Microsoft in a U.S. federal court. The lawsuit concerns the training and operation of generative AI systems such as ChatGPT and Microsoft Copilot. The New York Times claims these systems were trained using millions of copyrighted news articles without permission or payment.

What was the issue?

[Page 2]

Large language models are trained on vast datasets that include books, websites, and news articles.

The New York Times alleges that:

  • Its articles were included in these training datasets without a license.

  • AI systems can sometimes produce outputs that closely resemble or reproduce parts of its articles.

  • Users may obtain detailed summaries or content derived from Times articles without visiting the newspaper’s website.

From the newspaper’s perspective, this may:

  • Undermine its subscription model

  • Reduce web traffic

  • Allow other companies to profit from its journalism

The core legal question is whether using copyrighted material to train AI models constitutes copyright infringement or fair use under U.S. law.

<Image: AI model being trained on a pile of newspapers>

What has this got to do with AI?

[Page 3]

This case illustrates several risks associated with generative AI systems. First, modern AI models require enormous quantities of training data. Much of the publicly available data on the internet is protected by copyright. Second, generative models sometimes produce outputs that resemble or partially reproduce their training data. Third, the companies building AI systems are often different from the organisations that produced the original content.

These factors create tension between:

  • AI development

  • copyright law

  • the economic sustainability of content creators

The case therefore raises broader questions:

  • Should AI companies pay for training data?

  • Is training an AI system a “transformative” use of content?

  • Who benefits economically from AI-generated knowledge?

So how might the case resolve legally?

[Page 4]

Resolution (Ongoing)

As of 2026, the lawsuit has not yet reached a final judgment.

The defendants argue that training AI models on publicly available text qualifies as fair use, a doctrine in U.S. copyright law that permits limited use of copyrighted material without permission when the use is transformative.

The New York Times argues that:

  • The use is commercial

  • The scale of copying is enormous

  • The outputs may substitute for the original articles

The court will ultimately determine whether AI training practices violate copyright law or fall within existing legal protections.

Regardless of the outcome, the case may shape future rules governing AI training data.

[Page 5]

Takeaway

Modern AI systems depend on massive datasets drawn from the internet.

The NYT v. OpenAI case highlights the tension between:

  • technological innovation

  • intellectual property rights

  • economic incentives for content creation

As organisations increasingly deploy generative AI tools, existing legal frameworks will be tested.

The outcome of cases like this may determine:

  • how AI models are trained

  • whether licensing markets for training data emerge

  • how the benefits of AI are distributed across society
