If you open AI chatbot (such as ChatGPT, Gemini, etc.) and type:

Write me a 1000 word research report at a University level describing the fall of the Roman empire.

The AI can do this job, quickly and to a... moderately good standard. It can even add in-text-citations and a reference list if asked. Sometimes, the references might even be real!

As you can imagine, this has caused headaches for teachers. "Just write a 500 word essay" has been used for years as a stand-in for "do some independent research and practice your communication and thinking skills". Now that AI can do that with literally no intellectual effort on the part of the student, the question is what role does AI play in education and research now? We might want to take a step back and unpick some of our assumptions about what we are trying to achieve.

What is Education?

There are a lot of answers to this question, and some are probably longer than this entire course. Broadly we can break it down into a few categories of things.

Knowledge

The 'remembering of facts'

  • Do you know your times-tables?
  • The capital of New Zealand?

Skills

The capacity to do things.

  • Given an instruction can you complete it?
  • Do you know the process, practices and knowledge to achieve some outcome.

Values

Education can also instil values, such as:

  • Honesty
  • Rigour 
  • Empathy

Reasoning

There is a lot of things that can be lumped into this basket.

  • Do you understand why the facts you have learned are the way they are?
  • Do you know what they might further imply about the world? You might not know the capital of Uzbekistan, but you can probably reason that Uzbekistan probably has a capital.
  • You could lump in other similar skills like interpretation, abstraction, and analysis into this bucket (we are being a bit loose here).

Social

Education helps us learn how to live alongside other people and contribute positively to our society.

  • We practise communicating, collaborating, disagreeing respectfully, and participating in communities.
  • You can think of it as learning how to be part of something bigger than yourself, whether that’s a group assignment, a workplace, or society more broadly.

Critical

Education also gives us tools to question the world rather than just accept it.

  • Why are things the way they are?
  • Who benefits?
  • Who might be left out?
  • Developing a critical thinking lens helps us navigate complexity, recognise bias, and participate meaningfully in civic life—not just repeat information but examine it.

Now, the above list is basically a set of 'goals of education' (what we are trying to achieve). We can also consider the 'practice of education' (how we get there). It is important to understand that the advent of AI hasn't really changed any of the goals of education. We still strive to gain knowledge and develop our skills, reasoning and values. What AI has changed, dramatically, is the practice of education. What we do to achieve these goals.

What is Replaceable?

None of it. At least, not entirely.

Memorised knowledge has become less valuable, even before GenAI. Knowing where to find facts can be just as effective as knowing the same facts 'off the top of your head'. It is much more efficient to remember the location of a list of world capitals than to spend hours memorising it (and potentially making mistakes). Moreover, if you don't know enough about a subject, it is easy to be come paralysed, because you don't even know where to start or what questions to ask. What about skills? Like knowledge, at least some of it can be outsourced. Modern workplaces are collaborative environments and so a lot of skills are already outsourced. You can't do everything yourself. That said, if you don't have any skills, one might wonder how you got a job in the first place. What would you say... you do around here? We definitely need to preserve some skills.

What about reasoning then? If an AI-bot is doing your reasoning for you, how can you check if it makes sense unless you can reason well yourself? (Hint: You can't). Reasoning is its own kind of skill (a meta-skill if you will), which requires practice. Thus, when using AI in any capacity, we need to make sure that we aren't losing the 'reasoning practice'.

Hopefully, you are now convinced that the goals of education haven't really been changed by the advent of AI (yet), so the question then becomes. What should the practice of AI look like.

Case study

To see why reasoning can’t simply be outsourced to AI, consider one of the experiments described by Quattrociocchi (2026). In this study, 50 people and six leading LLMs were given a set of news headlines and asked to rate how credible each one was and justify their rating.

Here’s what typically happens when a person encounters something that feels a bit dodgy:

They check it against what they already know about the world. Does it fit with basic facts or past events?

They think about the source. Is this from a reputable outlet or from one known for exaggeration or bias?

They consider whether the headline actually makes sense in the broader scheme of things. Could this realistically have happened?

This is real reasoning. It’s slow, reflective and anchored in experience, memory and domain knowledge.

The LLMs, however, did something very different. Even when they reached the same final judgment as human participants, their explanations were nothing like human reasoning. They didn’t draw on background knowledge, lived experience or an understanding of how events typically unfold. Instead, their justifications were built from patterns in language, how often certain words appear together, which phrases tend to occur in credible contexts, and so on. They were using statistical echoes of credibility rather than the substance of it.

This gap is crucial. If we rely on AI to do our reasoning for us, we may get answers that sound sensible but aren’t grounded in the kinds of checks that humans naturally perform. It means that without our own reasoning skills, we can’t tell when an AI response is off‑track, because the tool itself doesn’t “know” anything in the human sense. It’s not verifying facts, evaluating plausibility or drawing on actual understanding. It’s predicting what a good answer looks like.

This case study reinforces the point: while parts of knowledge and skills can be supported by AI, reasoning cannot be replaced. At best, AI can imitate the shape of reasoning. It cannot perform the messy, experiential, grounded thinking that humans rely on to make sense of the world. And if we stop practising that ourselves, we risk losing the very thing we need to evaluate whether the AI is right in the first place.

What are the risks?

Like any powerful technology, AI comes with risks, both for how we learn and how we participate in education.

Offloading Learning

AI makes tasks easier, which sounds great, until it gets too easy. Cognitive psychology reminds us that we learn best when the work is a little bit hard. When an AI tool writes, analyses, or solves things for you, it can remove the struggle that actually builds memory and understanding. If we outsource too much thinking, we also risk weakening our ability to reason, recall, and problem‑solve independently.

Over‑reliance on Automation

AI can be wrong, biased, or misleading, yet it often sounds confident. If we get into the habit of trusting whatever the tool produces, we stop questioning it. This can erode critical thinking and make it harder to evaluate the quality of information, skills that remain essential regardless of how smart technology becomes.

Data Privacy

Most AI tools learn from the data we give them. When you upload an assignment draft, notes, or even your ideas, you may be handing that material to a company that can store it, analyse it, and use it to train future models. A quick example: Turnitin is extremely good at matching text because it has been trained on millions of student submissions (think about this next time you upload your assignments!). The same logic applies to generative AI; your work can become part of their training data.

Intellectual Property

When you put your writing, research, or creative work into an AI platform, you may be giving away rights to it (sometimes without realising). This includes your own intellectual property and, depending on what you upload, potentially the university’s as well. Always check what a tool claims it can store, use, or reuse.

Equity and Access

Some students have access to advanced paid AI tools, fast devices, and reliable internet. Others don’t. As AI becomes woven into learning, we need to consider whether it creates new inequities or widens existing gaps in who can participate fully.

Taken together, these risks don’t mean we should avoid AI entirely. But they do mean we need to use it thoughtfully. Keeping enough challenge in our learning, protecting our data, and continuing to question how these technologies shape our educational world.
