The History of AI

To really get your head around how AI works and the underlying assumptions and techniques baked into AI systems, it pays to take a step back and look at the broader context. Don't worry, we're not going to expect you to memorise dates or technical details (you can always ask an LLM if you need to). Instead, we are interested in what the strengths and limitations of technology was over time and how that was shaped by historical, technical and economic decisions.

Understanding this history will help you critically evaluate modern AI tools, especially when they are presented as neutral, inevitable, or universally beneficial.

A brief orientation before you start

As you work through the resources on this page, keep one question in mind:

What assumptions and trade-offs are built into AI systems from the time and context in which they were developed?

You will revisit this idea throughout the course.

Shift 1: The Origins of Artificial Intelligence

Early AI systems relied on explicitly programmed rules. These approaches worked well for narrow tasks but struggled in complex, real-world environments.

Shift 2: From Programming Rules to Learning Rules

By limiting AI to explicit rules, we limited AI to things we already well understood (calculators are good example). Even early on, the ideas behind Machine Learning (letting the machine learn the rules) already existed, but there wasn't enough computational power nor data to power this next revolution. Recent advances are driven as much by scale as by new ideas.

Shift 3: From Tools to Infrastructure

AI systems are now so widespread that they are nearly invisible, embedded in everyday tools from search engines and recommendation systems to digital assistants and online services.

These systems rely on continuous data collection to function and improve.

Connecting history to responsible AI use

AI systems are not neutral or timeless. They reflect the priorities, constraints, and assumptions of the contexts in which they were built.

As you move through this course, you will be asked to consider not just what AI can do, but whether it should be used in particular situations, and what evidence supports that decision.

Understanding the history of AI helps you recognise when claims about AI are oversimplified, overconfident, or missing important context.
