As we progress through our journey into the realm of Artificial Intelligence, we now confront its equivocal nature: the good, the bad, and the downright ugly. AI is not just a buzzword; it's a powerful force reshaping industries, societies, and our daily lives. But with great power comes great responsibility.

In this section, we step back from the excitement and explore AI through a critical lens. What are its benefits, and who benefits? What risks does it pose, and who bears the cost? And what deeper, structural consequences are unfolding beneath the surface?

Rather than offering simple answers, we’ll examine AI’s impact across three dimensions:

The Good

Where AI supports equity, creativity, sustainability, and access.

The Bad

Where AI introduces bias, surveillance, exploitation, and exclusion.

The Ugly

Where AI reinforces colonial logics, environmental harm, and systemic injustice.

This isn’t about deciding whether AI is “good” or “bad”, it’s about learning to ask better questions. As future professionals, educators, and citizens, your ability to critically evaluate AI will shape how you use it, challenge it, and imagine alternatives.

Prepare to engage, question, and reflect as we explore the multifaceted influences of AI. This is a week of discovery, critical thinking, and perhaps even a bit of discomfort as we confront the less glamorous sides of technology. Let's uncover the truths about AI together.

How does AI shape the world we live in: Who benefits? Who is harmed?

Choose a theme from the list below that is most relevant to your degree or future career. Read the Good/Bad/Ugly for that theme. In the workshop, you’ll build an example that may draw from a case study in your chosen area.

 

Theme A — Health, Wellbeing & Human Support

The Good

  • Earlier detection & personalised care: AI helps detect disease earlier and tailor treatments; in education contexts, adaptive tutors and translation tools personalise support for diverse learners.
  • Mental health support: Chatbots and virtual companions can provide low‑barrier support and triage (not a replacement for clinicians).
  • Accessibility & inclusion: Speech‑to‑text, image captioning, gesture recognition, and adaptive interfaces improve access for people with disability.
  • Reducing routine burdens: Automating scheduling, documentation, and other admin frees clinicians and educators to focus on relationships and pedagogy.

The Bad

  • Bias & misdiagnosis: Models trained on unrepresentative data can under‑serve non‑Western populations and linguistically diverse cohorts.
  • Emotional AI & biometrics: Facial/voice “emotion detection” and classroom/hospital monitoring risk misinterpretation, surveillance creep, and consent issues.
  • Overtrust & skill atrophy: Confident AI outputs can displace professional judgment; over‑reliance reduces practice in reasoning and decision‑making.
  • Opaque decisions in high‑stakes settings: Black‑box scoring (grades, triage, risk) undermines accountability and contestability.

The Ugly

  • Worker harm & hidden labour: Content moderation and data labelling expose low‑paid workers to trauma with limited support.
  • Mental health impacts at scale: Deepfakes and synthetic media fuel anxiety, identity confusion, and disinformation fatigue.
  • Intersectional harms: Impacts compound across race, gender, class, geography, and ability—those already marginalised often bear the greatest risk.

Theme B — Environment & Sustainability

The Good

  • Climate modelling & stewardship: AI helps model complex systems (oceans, carbon cycles), track wildlife, map ecosystems, and monitor pollution.
  • Smarter energy use: Home and grid‑level optimisation (e.g., delaying appliances to off‑peak, pre‑cooling with renewables) reduces cost and emissions; integrates with solar/batteries.
  • Disaster response: Analysing satellite, sensor, and social data supports rapid response and logistics during fires, floods, and humanitarian crises.
  • Sustainable production: AI can improve agricultural efficiency and resource use when aligned with sustainability goals.

The Bad

  • Efficiency over equity: Optimisation can allocate resources in ways that disadvantage certain regions or communities if justice isn’t explicit in the design.
  • Access gaps: Tools that enable “green” choices (smart devices, paid platforms) are unevenly available, reinforcing environmental inequities.

The Ugly

  • Material footprint: Training/serving large models consumes significant energy and water (e.g., GPT‑3 ≈ 1,200 MWh electricity and ~700,000 litres of cooling water), producing e‑waste and extraction pressures.
  • Uneven burdens: Environmental costs fall disproportionately on Indigenous and vulnerable communities near data centres, mines, and water‑stressed regions.
  • Infrastructure colonialism: Compute farms and energy‑intensive facilities are sited where labour and regulation are cheapest, exporting harms while centralising benefits.

Theme C — Law, Governance & Rights

The Good

  • Democratising expertise: AI can broaden access to legal information and policy literacy for individuals and small organisations.
  • Ethical AI movements & open‑source: Community‑governed tools prioritise transparency, fair labour, and Indigenous data sovereignty—offering alternatives to corporate control.
  • Decision support: Scenario modelling and ethics simulations help explore trade‑offs and improve deliberation in public policy.

The Bad

  • Surveillance & data extraction: Pervasive collection (scraping, passive tracking) normalises monitoring in education, health, employment, and public spaces.
  • Accountability gaps: Deployment often outpaces oversight; decisions are made by unaccountable actors with limited transparency.
  • Opaque/black‑box decisions: Grading, immigration, benefits, hiring—algorithmic determinations without clear reasons or appeal.
  • Regulatory lag & monopoly power: Laws trail practice; a handful of firms control models and infrastructure, entrenching dependency.

The Ugly

  • AI in warfare: Autonomous systems and targeting raise profound responsibility and escalation risks.
  • Manipulated discourse: Platforms and generative tools amplify and fabricate content; coordinated actors can poison RAG systems by flooding the web to skew “authoritative” answers.
  • Technocratic control & ideology: Metrics and optimisation values can override democratic aims; elite‑driven ideologies (e.g., TESCREAL) shape futures for the few, not the many.

Theme D — Society, Culture & Creativity

The Good

  • Creative amplification: Generative tools support music, writing, design, and rapid prototyping without replacing human creativity.
  • Linguistic diversity: Translation/localisation can preserve and amplify endangered languages and broaden access to services and culture.
  • Citizen science & participation: Communities collect and interpret data for biodiversity, urban planning, and local research.
  • Inclusive design: Captioning, alt‑text, and adaptive interfaces lower barriers to cultural participation.

The Bad

  • Epistemic gatekeeping: Western‑centric training data marginalises Indigenous, decolonial, and alternative knowledge systems—shaping what “counts” as knowledge.
  • Misinformation & scholarly pollution: Convincing fabrications (papers, citations, datasets) erode trust and quality.
  • Overtrust & access gaps: Plausible‑sounding outputs and uneven access to premium tools widen disparities in participation and influence.

The Ugly

  • Cultural appropriation: Models mimic Indigenous art, music, and literature without consent or context—commodifying heritage.
  • Scientific inequity: Wealthy institutions gain AI‑enhanced advantage; global knowledge production gaps widen.
  • Eroded public discourse: Algorithmic curation and deepfakes manipulate attention and identity, undermining democratic conversation and wellbeing.

Theme E — Work, Labour & the Economy

The Good

  • Productivity & focus: Automating routine tasks (scheduling, data entry, first‑pass grading) frees time for complex, relational work.
  • Levelling the field: Access to coding assistance, design tools, and analysis can empower small firms and entrepreneurs.
  • Open innovation: Open‑source ecosystems reduce costs, enable scrutiny, and diversify participation.

The Bad

  • Job displacement & deskilling: Automation replaces roles and shifts expertise from people to machines, narrowing human judgement to oversight.
  • Workplace surveillance: Biometric/behavioural tracking justifies “productivity” controls and erodes autonomy.
  • Vendor lock‑in: Dependence on proprietary platforms limits transparency and strategic flexibility.
  • Digital divide: Premium tools outperform free versions, advantaging those who can pay.

The Ugly

  • Labour exploitation: Low‑paid annotators and moderators sustain AI systems while absorbing harms.
  • Infrastructure colonialism in supply chains: Data centres and component manufacturing cluster where labour is cheap and regulation lax.
  • Management by metrics: AI‑driven optimisation reconfigures organisations around surveillance and compliance rather than care and judgement.

The good

Despite its risks, AI offers powerful opportunities to improve lives, expand access, and support human creativity. When designed and deployed ethically, AI can be a force for good, enhancing equity, sustainability, and innovation across sectors. The number of positive applications is almost endless. Here are a few to get you thinking.

Democratising Expertise

AI can make expert-level tools and insights available to non-experts. From legal advice to coding assistance, users can access support that would otherwise require specialised training or expensive services. This can empower individuals and small organisations to solve problems, innovate, and participate more fully in digital economies.


AI can help bridge educational gaps by offering personalised learning support, translation tools, and adaptive content. Students with diverse needs, languages, or learning styles can access tailored resources that meet them where they are. In remote or under-resourced areas, AI tutors and open-source platforms can offer learning opportunities that were previously out of reach.

Optimising Energy Use in Smart Homes

AI can help households reduce energy costs and environmental impact by intelligently managing appliances based on real-time electricity prices and grid demand. For example, a smart home system powered by AI might delay running the dishwasher until off-peak hours or pre-cool a house when renewable energy is abundant. These systems can learn user habits, respond to weather forecasts, and integrate with solar panels or battery storage to maximise efficiency. By automating energy decisions, AI makes sustainable living more accessible and affordable.

Supporting Creativity and Innovation

Generative AI tools enable users to create music, art, writing, and design in ways that were once limited by technical skill or access to resources. Artists can experiment with new forms, writers can overcome creative blocks, and designers can prototype ideas rapidly. AI doesn’t replace creativity; it can amplify it.

Improving Health and Well-being

AI systems are being used to detect diseases earlier, personalise treatment plans, and support mental health through virtual assistants and chatbots. In public health, AI can model disease spread, optimise resource allocation, and support emergency response. These tools have the potential to save lives and improve care, especially in underserved communities.

Enhancing Accessibility and Inclusivity 

AI-powered tools like speech-to-text, image captioning, and real-time translation improve accessibility for people with disabilities. These technologies help break down barriers in communication, education, and employment, supporting more inclusive environments. AI is helping designers create more inclusive environments, whether through automated captioning, gesture recognition, or adaptive interfaces. These innovations support people with disabilities in navigating digital and physical spaces more independently and confidently.

Reducing Routine Burdens

AI can automate repetitive tasks, like scheduling, data entry, or grading, freeing up time for more meaningful work. In education, this can allow teachers to focus on relationships and pedagogy. In healthcare, it can reduce administrative load and improve patient care. When used thoughtfully, automation can support, not replace, human labour.

Fostering Linguistic Diversity

AI-powered translation tools can support endangered languages and amplify linguistic diversity. By enabling real-time translation and localisation, AI can help preserve cultural heritage and improve access to education, health, and legal services in communities that have historically been excluded from dominant language systems.

Disaster Response and Humanitarian Aid

AI can analyse satellite imagery, social media, and sensor data to coordinate emergency responses during natural disasters or humanitarian crises. It can help identify affected areas, optimise logistics, and predict secondary risks, saving lives and improving aid delivery in real time.

Citizen Science and Participatory Research

AI tools are increasingly used in citizen science projects, allowing communities to collect, analyse, and interpret data, from biodiversity monitoring to urban planning. This democratises research and empowers local knowledge holders to contribute meaningfully to scientific inquiry.

Ethical AI Movements and Open-Source Innovation

The rise of open-source AI communities offers alternatives to corporate monopolies. These projects prioritise transparency, community governance, and ethical design, creating tools that reflect diverse values and resist extractive logics. Ethical AI movements also advocate for inclusive datasets, fair labour practices, and Indigenous data sovereignty.

Mental Health Support and Social Connection

AI chatbots and virtual companions are being used to support mental health, especially in contexts where access to human therapists is limited. While not a replacement for professional care, these tools can offer companionship, crisis support, and early intervention, particularly for isolated or vulnerable individuals.

Climate Modeling and Environment Stewardship

AI is being used to model complex environmental systems, from ocean currents to carbon cycles. These models help scientists understand climate dynamics, predict future scenarios, and design more effective mitigation strategies. AI also supports conservation efforts by tracking wildlife, detecting illegal logging, and mapping ecosystems.  AI can support climate action by modelling environmental changes, optimising energy use, and improving disaster response. It can help track deforestation, monitor pollution, and design more efficient systems for agriculture and transport. When aligned with sustainability goals, AI can be a valuable ally in protecting the planet.

Supporting Ethical Decision-Making

AI can be used to simulate ethical dilemmas, support deliberative processes, and model the consequences of policy decisions. In education, law, and governance, these tools can help users explore complex moral questions and reflect on values, when designed with transparency and care.

 

The bad

 While AI is often celebrated for its efficiency and innovation, its deployment can produce serious harms, some visible, others deeply embedded in the systems we trust. These aren’t just technical glitches; they reflect structural problems in how AI is designed, governed, and used.

Bias and Discimination

AI systems are trained on data that reflects the world as it is, not as it should be. This means they often reproduce and amplify existing social biases. From misdiagnosing illnesses in non-Western populations to unfairly grading students from diverse linguistic backgrounds, AI can entrench discrimination in ways that are hard to detect and harder to challenge. AI doesn’t just reflect bias, it shapes what counts as knowledge. Generative models are trained on Western-centric data, marginalising Indigenous, decolonial, and alternative epistemologies. This creates a form of epistemic gatekeeping, where dominant perspectives are amplified and others are erased.

Surveillance and Data Extraction

AI thrives on data. But much of this data is collected without consent, scraped from the internet or harvested through passive monitoring. In education, health, and employment, AI systems increasingly track behaviour, interactions, and even emotions, turning users into data points for corporate profit. This normalises surveillance and undermines privacy.

Governance and Accountability

AI systems are often deployed without meaningful public oversight. Decisions about how AI is built, trained, and used are typically made by a small group of developers, corporate leaders, or policymakers, many of whom are not accountable to the communities affected. This lack of democratic governance means that AI can be used to enforce policies, shape public services, or influence behaviour without transparency or consent. Without robust accountability mechanisms, AI risks becoming a tool of unchecked power.

Overtrust and Automation Dependence

AI systems often present outputs with confidence, even when they're wrong. Users (students, doctors, educators) may defer to these outputs, trusting the machine over their own judgment. This can lead to poor decisions, reduced critical thinking, and a dangerous reliance on systems that lack transparency.

Cognitive Offloading and Skill Atrophy

As AI becomes a default tool for writing, problem-solving, and decision-making, users may begin to outsource their thinking. Students rely on AI to draft essays, professionals use it to summarise reports, and even educators turn to it for lesson planning. Over time, this can lead to a decline in critical thinking, creativity, and independent reasoning, skills that education is meant to cultivate.

Scientific Fabrication and Misinformation

AI can generate convincing but entirely fabricated research papers, citations, and datasets. In academic settings, this undermines scholarly integrity and pollutes the knowledge ecosystem. The ease of generating “plausible” content makes it harder to distinguish genuine scholarship from synthetic noise.

Biometric Surveillance and Emotional AI

AI systems increasingly use facial recognition, voice analysis, and emotion detection to monitor users. These technologies are often deployed in classrooms, workplaces, or public spaces without informed consent. The result is a new form of biometric surveillance, one that tracks not just what we do, but how we feel.

Opaque Decision-Making

Many AI systems operate as black boxes. They make decisions about grades, diagnoses, or job applications, without revealing how or why. This lack of explainability undermines accountability and makes it difficult to challenge errors or biases, especially in high-stakes contexts.

Legal and Regulatory Gaps

AI is evolving faster than the laws that govern it. In many jurisdictions, there are no clear rules about data use, algorithmic accountability, or user rights. This regulatory vacuum allows companies to deploy risky systems with minimal oversight, leaving users vulnerable to harm.

Corporate Control and Monopoly

A handful of tech giants dominate AI infrastructure, creating barriers to entry for ethical or community-led alternatives. Individuals, corporations, universities and governments become dependent on proprietary platforms, limiting transparency and reinforcing market control.

Digital Divide

Access to AI tools is uneven. Rural, remote, and low-income communities often lack the infrastructure or resources to benefit. Premium tools offer better performance, while free versions may be biased or limited—deepening global and local inequities.

Job Displacement

While AI can reduce routine burdens, it also threatens to displace workers across industries. Roles in customer service, education, logistics, and even creative fields are increasingly automated, often without adequate support for retraining or transition. At the same time, AI can deskill professions by shifting expertise from humans to machines—reducing autonomy, flattening judgment, and reshaping labour into oversight of algorithmic systems.

The ugly

 Beyond the immediate harms, AI carries deeper, systemic consequences that are harder to see but no less urgent. These are the “ugly” truths that challenge the very idea of justice in an AI-driven world.

Environmental Impact

AI is not immaterial. We cover this in detail on the next page, but briefly, training and running large models require enormous energy and water. For example, GPT-3 consumed over 1,200 MWh of electricity and used around 700,000 litres of water for cooling. In regions already facing droughts, these demands exacerbate climate vulnerabilities, often affecting the very communities AI claims to help.

Even when powered by renewables, AI infrastructure still contributes to e-waste, carbon emissions, and resource extraction. The environmental cost is global, but its impacts are uneven, falling hardest on marginalised and Indigenous communities whose lands and waters bear the burden.

AI in Warfare and Autonomous Weapons

AI is increasingly used in military contexts, from autonomous drones to satellite surveillance. These systems promise speed and precision, but they also raise profound ethical questions. Who is accountable when an AI system makes a lethal mistake? And what happens when machines can escalate conflict faster than diplomacy can respond?

AI and Public Discourse

AI plays a growing role in shaping what we see, read, and believe. Algorithms curate news feeds, amplify certain voices, and suppress others—often based on engagement metrics rather than truth or relevance. Generative AI can produce synthetic media, deepfakes, and persuasive content at scale, influencing public opinion and political narratives. This raises urgent questions about misinformation, manipulation, and the erosion of democratic discourse.

Mental Health Impacts

AI-generated content, especially deepfakes and synthetic media, can contribute to anxiety, disinformation fatigue, and identity confusion. Meanwhile, the workers who moderate AI content are often exposed to graphic and traumatic material, with little psychological support. The mental health toll of AI is real, but rarely acknowledged.

Infrastructure Colonialism

AI infrastructure, data centres, compute farms, and energy-intensive servers are often built in regions with cheap labour, lax regulation, or vulnerable ecosystems. This mirrors historical patterns of extraction, where the benefits flow upward and the harms stay local.

Scientific Inequity

Access to AI-enhanced research tools is uneven. Elite institutions and wealthy nations can afford cutting-edge models, while others are left behind. This widens the gap in global knowledge production and reinforces academic hierarchies.

Cultural Appropriation by AI

Generative models can mimic Indigenous art, music, and literature, often without consent or context. This commodifies cultural expression and strips it of meaning, turning heritage into content. Without safeguards, AI risks becoming a tool of cultural erasure. Feeding cultural archives into AI risks stripping communities of control over their own stories, a form of digital colonialism.

Labour Exploitation

Behind the sleek interfaces of AI are thousands of low-paid workers who label data and moderate harmful content. Many work under exploitative conditions, exposed to traumatic material with little support. The human cost of AI development is often hidden, but it's real.

Technocratic Control

AI doesn't just automate tasks; it reconfigures how decisions are made and who holds power (the few behind the AI systems). Across sectors, AI systems are increasingly embedded into bureaucratic and organisational processes, aligning operations with metrics of efficiency, optimisation, and control. Whether in government, healthcare, education, or corporate environments, AI is being used to streamline workflows, predict behaviour, and enforce compliance, not necessarily to empower people.

Those behind this decision-making and the AI tools wield large amounts of power over who can access AI and how the systems are configured (e.g., which political views are incorporated). Emerging reporting even suggests that multiple state and non-state actors are beginning to explicitly target retrieval-augmented generation systems by saturating the web with strategically aligned content, raising new concerns about the political economy of ‘authoritative’ AI knowledge.

AI Ideologies

AI is not neutral. It is shaped by ideologies of those behind it that prioritise optimisation, control, and hierarchy, often echoing eugenic and fascist logics. Some leading AI voices promote worldviews (known as TESCREAL) where futures are designed by and for a select elite, where rationality and efficiency override care, plurality, and justice. For example, Musk’s promotion of “anti-woke” AI demonstrates how narratives of far-right politics are easily entangled in these tools. The rapid release of increasingly powerful gen-AI models, often without adequate safeguards or regulation, exemplifies this ethos.

Intersectional Harms

AI does not impact all people equally. Its harms are shaped by race, gender, class, geography, and ability. For example, facial recognition systems often misidentify people of colour, predictive policing disproportionately targets marginalised communities, and access to AI tools is stratified by income and infrastructure. Understanding AI’s impact requires an intersectional lens—one that recognises how multiple forms of inequality are reproduced and intensified through technology.
