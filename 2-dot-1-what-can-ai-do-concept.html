Traditional AI

It is important to take a step back and realise that not all Artificial Intelligence systems are the same. For example, you can program an AI to play chess (such as Deep Blue - Figure 1). This kind of AI might be better thought of as artificial "logical thinking". Deep Blue considers possible chess moves and tries to evaluate whether it thinks they are good or bad. This isn't really dissimilar to how a person plays chess: consider a move, see how it might play out, compare it to other moves.

There are many AI systems which operate using similar logic. The thermostat on your air-conditioner (a very primitive AI) turns on the air-conditioner if it is too hot, and turns on the heater if it is too cold (hopefully not both at the same time). The numbers for too-hot and too-cold can be set manually and detected by a temperature sensor. These kinds of AI systems are generally pretty trustworthy, mostly because we understand their decisions quite explicitly (in general they have been written directly by humans and replicate human decision making).

The Shortfall of 'Traditional AI'

This activity explores the difference between making accurate predictions and understanding how those predictions are made.

Imagine you are part of a research lab studying how intelligent systems learn to recognise objects.

The team has built a classifier trained to distinguish between blueberry muffins and chihuahuas. It learns from examples and feedback, gradually improving its accuracy. Over time, it becomes remarkably good at the task.

But something puzzles the researchers.

  • They can observe the system improving.
  • They can measure how often it is correct.
  • They can see how it changes when given more information.

Yet they cannot clearly explain what it has learned internally or exactly which features drive its decisions.

The system works. But its knowledge is not organised in ways humans naturally understand.

You are about to take part in a simplified version of this experiment.

You will classify images that begin partially obscured and gradually become clearer. As more visual information becomes available, you may revise your decisions.

As you work, pay attention to two things:

  • how your accuracy changes as the images become clearer
  • how your ability to explain your decisions changes

The goal is not just to see whether you are correct, but to notice the gap between recognising something and explaining why

What the experiment reveals

As the images became clearer, your classifications likely became more accurate.
With more visual information, you were able to refine your decisions.

This shows how performance can improve when more information becomes available.

However, improvement alone does not mean the task becomes easy to explain.

Even when you recognised the correct category, it may have been difficult to describe exactly why, or to create a rule that works for every image.

In other words, making a decision and explaining that decision are not always the same thing.

This activity highlights the difference between:

  • recognising patterns

  • explaining how those patterns are recognised

This distinction is important because modern AI systems also learn to make classifications by detecting patterns in data.

Why this matters

Many real-world classification tasks involve subtle or overlapping features.

In these situations, decisions are often based on combinations of visual cues rather than a single clear rule.

As you experienced in this activity, it is possible to make accurate judgements while still finding it difficult to explain exactly how those judgements were made.

AI systems that learn from large amounts of data face similar kinds of pattern-based classification tasks.

Understanding how recognition and explanation can behave differently helps us understand how these systems operate.

Key Takeaway

Accurate classification can improve with more information even when the rules behind those decisions remain difficult to explain, which is also true of many AI systems.

Looking ahead

The experience you just had raises an important question:

What does it mean for an intelligent system to learn from patterns when the basis of its decisions is not always easy to explain?

If you found the task particularly difficult, don't worry. This is because, we don't really understand how we see objects and so for a very long time, this task was almost impossible for computers. Machine Learning

So, we've already determined that telling a muffin from a dog is quite easy, but explaining why is hard. This led to the development of machine learning. So what is the difference between artificial intelligence and machine learning? In machine learning, we know the output we want (dog/muffin) and we know what the input is (image of a dog or muffin) - (Figure 3). What machine learning algorithms do, is allow a computer to follow some 'high level rules' about learning. The machine guesses the muffin is a dog (a wrong answer) and programmers design algorithms that tell the machine how to get the answer 'less wrong' until it gets it right. If you have enough pictures of dogs and muffins, the program will eventually be able to tell the two apart. There is obviously a lot of mathematics involved, but that isn't what is important here. What matters is the following:

We understand how the AI learns.

We don't understand what the AI learns.

After we have trained the AI, it may be very, very accurate (hooray). However, we often don't really understand why it is so accurate. For any given picture of a dog, the AI is performing many calculations (sometimes millions) to eventually get to the answer 'dog' and that maths is basically more complex than a person can really wrap their head around (the individual equations aren't too bad, but there are millions of them). Machine learning algorithms, are a big black box. We'll learn in later topics, that this is the root cause of all the problems.

Interactive visualisation from Adam Harley

This interactive visualisation from Adam Harley shows how a simple machine learning system processes information to recognise handwritten numbers. You can draw a digit and watch how patterns of activation move through multiple layers before the system produces an output at the top of the screen. As you interact with the visualisation, pay attention to what happens as your drawing moves through the network:

  • how your rough sketch is gradually broken down and rebuilt into patterns the system can work with
  • how quickly the process stops making intuitive sense, even though you can still see what is happening
  • how certain the final answer looks, even when your drawing is messy or ambiguous

You are not expected to understand every layer or calculation. The key idea is that even when we can see inside a machine learning system, it can still be hard to explain why it reaches a particular decision. This is what is meant by the “black box” problem in AI.

Note that nothing is actually hidden (i.e., it is not really a black box), but that understanding the low-level mechanical processes does not automatically give us confidence in the reasoning.

Recognising this limitation is essential when deciding whether an AI system should, or should not, be trusted in real-world contexts. Being able to say “this looks impressive, but I cannot explain how it reached this conclusion or whether it is appropriate to use here” is a core part of responsible AI practice.

Generative AI

There is one extra step we need to cover before we get to the kinds of 'AI' that people have 'strong opinions' about. We've shown that if you have an input and you know the output you want, you can pretty much train an artificial intelligence to anything as long as you can formulate your problem correctly. One famous problem that it has taken us a long time to solve, is that of the 'chatbot'. Can you make an AI that can talk like a person? This is the topic of the famous 'Turing Test', posed by Alan Turing, the 'father' of modern computer science (Figure 5).

So how do tools like ChatGPT work? They use machine learning, but that isn't really enough to understand what they are doing. We need to look more closely at the problem they are trying to solve. In this case, they are trained, one word at a time. At any given point in time, every Large Language Model (LLM) is taking the current conversation (what you have said and what they have said) and working out... which word should come next? This sounds a bit incredible, but with enough data and enough training you end up with ChatGPT, Gemini and Grok.

Ideally, you don't want the AI to say the same thing all the time (that is hardly realistic) and so you'll need to add in some randomness. We're going to learn very quickly that it is this randomness which is going to cause its own set of problems.

So, What can Artificial Intelligence do?

Almost anything? If you understand a problem very well (like chess), you can use old-school formal logic. If you have a lot of labeled data (like pictures with captions), you can use machine learning techniques to build yourself a machine to label pictures, recognise voices, detect cancer or choose the next song to play. Finally, if you have a lot of data (actually, a great deal of data) you can make a system capable of generative 'new' things based on human-like instructions.

I say 'you' here a lot, but the reality is that most people will never build their own AI system. Instead, almost everyone interacts with AI on a daily basis (whether they like it or not). One of the big limitations one what an AI can do is the person using it. The more experience you have with these systems, the more they can be used (for good or ill).
