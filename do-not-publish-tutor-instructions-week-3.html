Task #2: AI in research

Find an assignment that you have already completed (it must be from a prior semester or high school - not part of a course you are doing now). It can be any kind of assessment, but preferably a report or other digital artefact. 

Select an AI tool suitable for evaluating the work and providing feedback. Use the AI tool to generate feedback and suggestions on what the AI thinks should be changed or improved. In doing so, consider the following things.

  • Is the feedback helpful?
  • Is the feedback vague/precise? (can you change this?)
  • Is the feedback correct/incorrect? (can you verify this?)
    • If you were to change your work based on the AI's advice, would that be acceptable?
    • How does getting this kind of feedback differ from getting 'spelling and grammar' advice from a tool like Grammarly AI?
    • Does disclosing the use of AI change your position?
    • How do you feel about the university using AI to mark your assignments?

Task #4: Discussion

Overview

Run a structured discussion in small groups on questions about how AI might impact the curriculum, then debrief to the whole class. This bespoke app - Curriculum Lab lets you assign discussion questions to student tables and push them in real time to student devices.

 

- Allocate a number (from 1-9) to each table of students -

 

Quick setup

  • Live in class: Open the app link in a new browser tab and project the student view on screen.
  • Pre-prepared: Download the QR code from the app and add it to your PowerPoint slides before class so students can scan and join instantly.

How to use

  1. Based on the actual number of tables you're dealing with in the class, set the number of tables in the Tutor Dashboard.
  2. Click Randomise & Push to randomly assign questions, or manually select a question for each table and click Push.
  3. Students open the app on their device (via QR code or link), enter their table number, and see their assigned question with discussion prompts.

Discussion questions

The app will randomly allocate one of these questions to each group, or you can do it manually:

  1. Consider the degree you are studying: are any aspects of the content that is taught in your subject going to become less useful because of AI?
  2. Should the English writing and reading curriculum change now that AI is here? In what ways?
  3. Should language learning be more focused on intercultural skills rather than linguistics? What are the reasons for your answers?
  4. How might the presence of AI affect what is taught in visual arts curricula? Or music courses? What are the reasons for your answers?
  5. Are there any subjects or parts of the curriculum where students should deliberately learn without AI? Why or why not?
  6. Should schools and universities spend less time teaching students to memorise information, and more time teaching them to verify information? Why or why not?
  7. Would employers prefer to see a graduate from a university that encouraged students to use AI tools, or a university that banned the use of AI tools? What reasons do you have?
  8. How might AI impact what is taught in the computer science curriculum?
  9. If students are encouraged to use AI as part of their learning, should they be marked on how well they use AI? If so, what criteria and assessment methods should be used for this?

Structured discussion prompts

The app displays these prompts at the bottom of the student view — ask students to follow them as they engage with their question:

  1. Think — Think of your own individual answer.
  2. Share — Take turns sharing.
  3. Find the tension — What is changing? What should stay? Where is the disagreement?
  4. Agree — Agree on your best group response/s.
  5. Justify — Decide on clear justifications/reasons for your response/s.
