Scenario 1: Sound Of A City, Translating Place Into Experience Relevant Disciplines: Design Creative Arts Tourism Urban Design Cultural Studies Project Type: Experience Design Place-Based Storytelling Artefact Portfolio

Guiding Question
How can the identity of a real place be translated into a multisensory artistic experience?

Why This Matters
Cities and places can struggle to communicate identity, culture, history, and belonging. This has relevance to tourism, urban design, community, cultural preservation, Indigenous knowledge, and place-based storytelling.

Possible Approach

  • identify the stakeholders for your work
  • select a real location in South Australia
  • create a coherent artistic experience through sound, narrative, and visuals
  • use environmental sounds, rhythms of movement, textures, maps, memories, or imagined futures as inputs
  • ground the work in observation, research, or collected artefacts

Possible Deliverables

  • integrated experience, such as a video, installation concept, or live presentation
  • artefact portfolio of raw inputs, such as photos, recordings, notes, or interviews
  • design rationale and reflection showing how the elements connect
Scenario 2: Emotions Made Tangible, Designing A Human Experience Relevant Disciplines: Psychology Education Creative Arts Media Museum Studies Project Type: Experience Design Emotion Mapping Prototype Experience

Guiding Question
Can you design an artistic experience that makes people feel a specific emotion in a controlled way?

Why This Matters
This has relevance to education, immersive media, museum design, training simulations, and carefully framed wellbeing or therapeutic contexts.

Possible Approach

  • identify stakeholders and real-life spaces where such an experience could be useful
  • choose at least one emotion, such as anxiety under pressure, nostalgia, isolation, anticipation, or collective joy
  • design an emotional journey using sound, pacing, narrative structure, ambiguity, clarity, and visuals
  • decide whether the experience is linear, fragmented, immersive, or interactive

Possible Deliverables

  • experience prototype, such as a video, interactive concept, or live demonstration
  • timeline or map of intended feelings
  • supporting artefacts and reflection
Scenario 3: Reimagine A Future Human-Technology Relationship Relevant Disciplines: Philosophy Ethics Technology Psychology Policy Project Type: Future Scenario Ethical Analysis System Design

Guiding Question
What should the relationship between humans and a specific technology look like in 5 to 10 years, and how do we design for it now?

Why This Matters
We are reshaping relationships with AI, XR, wearables, health monitoring, and other decision-making systems, often faster than we understand their human consequences. This topic connects to philosophy, ethics, technology, psychology, design, and policy.

Possible Approach

  • choose one domain, such as education, healthcare, work, or relationships
  • imagine a future human-technology relationship and design a prototype or experience that embodies it
  • critically evaluate ethical, social, and operational implications
  • consider who decides what, where human oversight sits, and how the future state could realistically be reached

Possible Deliverables

  • future scenario
  • relationship model
  • prototype, interaction, simulation, or detailed concept
  • ethical and social implications analysis
  • transition pathway from present to future

Scenario 5: Designing An AI-Supported Workplace Safety System Relevant Disciplines: Engineering Psychology Business Workplace Relations Health Project Type: System Design Risk Analysis Human-AI Interaction

Guiding Question
How can AI be introduced into a frontline workplace without reducing worker trust or safety?

Why This Matters
Getting this right affects worker wellbeing, organisational performance, and whether AI is accepted or resisted in critical industries. This is relevant to engineering, psychology, business, labour relations, design, manufacturing, mining, logistics, healthcare, unions, and workplace policy.

Possible Approach

  • identify stakeholders, such as workers, supervisors, unions, safety regulators, and technology providers
  • select a real or plausible workplace context
  • map workflows, identify risks, and analyse current processes
  • define system inputs, outputs, decision boundaries, and escalation paths
  • consider surveillance concerns, false positives and negatives, worker resistance, trust, and implementation feasibility

Possible Deliverables

  • system design, such as architecture and workflows
  • scenario-based walkthrough
  • risk and ethics analysis
  • implementation plan
  • prototype dashboard or model
  • design rationale and reflection
Scenario 6: Reducing Urban Heat In A City Precinct Relevant Disciplines: Urban Planning Environmental Science Architecture Public Health Infrastructure Project Type: Environmental Design Spatial Analysis Feasibility Planning

Guiding Question
How can a neighbourhood be redesigned to reduce extreme heat exposure for residents?

Why This Matters
Extreme heat is one of the most immediate and dangerous impacts of climate change in cities. Addressing it can improve public health, equity, accessibility, and liveability. This topic is relevant to urban planning, climate adaptation, environmental science, architecture, public health, infrastructure, and data-informed design.

Possible Approach

  • identify stakeholders, such as residents, councils, urban planners, and affected population groups
  • select a real location in South Australia
  • assess stakeholder needs, heat exposure, usability, accessibility, cost, and policy fit
  • use AI to support environmental design decisions related to shading, vegetation, materials, airflow, or modelling options

Possible Deliverables

  • precinct redesign, such as maps, plans, or visuals
  • before and after comparison
  • feasibility analysis
  • implementation plan
  • design rationale and reflection

  • map product flow and identify waste points
  • explore how AI could support forecasting, inventory management, or redistribution
  • consider pricing, nudging, trust, supplier relationships, and business constraints

Possible Deliverables

  • system model showing flows and interventions
  • scenario walkthrough
  • economic and operational analysis
  • implementation plan
  • design rationale and reflection
Scenario 8: Designing An Ethical AI Hiring System Relevant Disciplines: Computer Science HR Law Ethics Organisational Practice Project Type: Governance Design Bias Analysis System Prototype

Guiding Question
How can organisations use AI in recruitment without introducing bias or discrimination?

Why This Matters
AI is increasingly used in hiring decisions, but it can reinforce bias, exclude qualified candidates, and damage trust. Fairness and transparency are critical for legal compliance, credibility, and equitable opportunity.

  • select a real or plausible hiring context
  • analyse the recruitment process and identify where bias could occur
  • define where human oversight sits, how decisions can be challenged, and how compliance is enforced

Possible Deliverables

  • hiring system model
  • bias and ethics analysis
  • governance, transparency, and oversight framework
  • candidate appeal process
  • prototype system
