Case Studies

The following four case studies are designed to help you critically examine how AI technologies are being adopted across diverse sectors—from climate resilience and healthcare to education and defence. Each case presents a real-world scenario where AI offers both promise and peril, highlighting the complex ethical, social, and technical tensions that arise in practice.

As you read, pay close attention to the “good,” “bad,” and “ugly” dimensions of AI in each context. These narratives are not just about what AI can do, but about what it should do and under what conditions.

Each case concludes with a set of reflection questions. These are designed to prompt deeper thinking. You may wish to take notes to help guide your contributions to the workshop.

Case Study: “The Rising Tide and the Algorithm”

In 2027, Pacific Horizons, a not-for-profit environmental organisation headquartered in Suva, Fiji, found itself at a strategic crossroads. Since its founding in 2003, the organisation’s mission had been clear: mitigate the effects of climate change on Pacific Island nations, with a focus on the slow but relentless rise of sea levels that was swallowing coastlines, farmlands, and entire communities. Over the years, they had run disaster preparedness programs, influenced regional policy, and documented traditional ecological knowledge from elders to inform adaptation strategies.

The team at Pacific Horizons had always relied on grassroots strategies—community workshops, regional policy advocacy, and traditional ecological knowledge shared by elders. But the climate crisis was accelerating. Cyclones had become more intense, flooding more frequent, and saltwater intrusion into freshwater aquifers more severe. In the last two years alone, four island communities had been forced into partial relocation. “We’re running out of time”, said the organisation’s director, Merewai Tawake, during a strategy meeting “Our current methods are too slow to match the pace of the crisis.”

That’s when Arjun Mehta, a young volunteer data scientist from New Zealand, suggested something radical: integrating AI into Pacific Horizons’ operations.

Case Study: “The Campus Knows Your Name”

In 2025, Northbridge University, a mid-sized institution in Australia, launched a bold initiative to integrate generative AI across its teaching, learning, and administrative systems. The project, called EduMorph, promised to personalise education, streamline assessment, and enhance student support through AI-powered tools.

At first, the results were impressive. Students received tailored feedback on essays within seconds. AI tutors helped clarify complex concepts in real time. Administrative staff used predictive analytics to identify students at risk of dropping out, enabling early intervention. “We’re finally delivering education at scale, with care,” said Vice Chancellor Dr. Marla Chen.

But as EduMorph expanded, so did the unease.

How the scenarios were created

Portions of these case studies were developed with the assistance of OpenAI’s GPT-5 language model via the Adelaide University Co-Pilot interface. The AI tool was used to help generate narrative structure, integrate relevant academic literature, and refine clarity and cohesion in the presentation of complex themes. All content was reviewed, edited, and fact-checked by the author, and final responsibility for the accuracy, interpretation, and conclusions rests solely with the author. Prompt text that was input into the AI

Role play as an academic colleague helping me write a realistic, discussion-ready case study for a university course on AI, aimed at a diverse audience with different levels of technical expertise.

  1. Scenario creation – Invent a fictional but plausible environmental NGO based in the Pacific Islands, including its history, mission, and current climate change challenges. Make it culturally respectful and grounded in real-world climate issues (e.g., sea-level rise, cyclones, freshwater intrusion).
  2. Positive AI potential – Using the referenced source material below, identify 4–5 specific ways generative AI could help this NGO achieve its mission. Use examples supported by credible references (APA 7th style), drawing on topics like predictive modelling, resource optimisation, design innovation, translation for language inclusion, and scaled community engagement.
  3. Risks and ethical concerns – Using the referenced source material below, integrate 3–4 thematic “concerns” that the NGO’s board might raise about adopting AI. Connect each concern explicitly to climate justice and Pacific community contexts. Explicit focus should be on the environment, social justice, First Nations perspective, and global AI divides
  4. Dilemma framing – Present the board’s deliberations as a conflict of values, not just technology. Show both cautious adoption advocates (with safeguards) and those opposed on ethical grounds.
  5. Reflection questions – Create 4–5 open-ended, thought-provoking questions to guide student discussion, ensuring they address environmental impact, social equity, and Indigenous data sovereignty.
  6. Tone and style – Write in clear, professional narrative prose, suitable for students from multiple disciplines. Keep it engaging while maintaining academic credibility.
  7. AI acknowledgement – Add an acknowledgement to the end of the case study explaining how AI was used in its creation.

Output the complete case study in sections: Scenario, Promise of AI, Concerns, The Dilemma, Reflection Questions, and References. All facts must be cited, with APA 7th style in-text citations and a full reference list.

Example of the referenced Source Material that was uploaded to the prompt:

Sustainability 

  • Disproportionate environmental impacts on marginalised communities – Affluent regions benefit economically and technologically from generative AI, while environmental and societal costs—such as water shortages, e-waste accumulation, and climate displacement—disproportionately affect vulnerable populations, exacerbating existing inequalities (Wright et al., 2023).
  • Decolonial perspective – Sustainability impacts of AI are often borne most heavily by Indigenous peoples due to environmental damage to Country (Worrell, 2024).
  • Water consumption for AI model training – Training GPT-3 required approximately 700,000 litres of water, enough to sustain a small community for weeks (Li et al., 2023).
  • Projected global AI water withdrawals – Broader AI demand could require 4.2–6.6 billion m³ of water annually by 2027, exceeding the total yearly water use of countries such as Denmark or equalling half that of the United Kingdom (Li et al., 2023).
  • Water scarcity context – Two-thirds of the global population already experiences annual water stress, a situation expected to worsen under climate change (Li et al., 2023).
  • Energy consumption of AI – Training GPT-3 at Microsoft’s data centres used an estimated 1,287 MWh (Patterson et al., 2021) or 552 metric tons of CO₂ emissions (Tomlinson et al., 2023), equivalent to the annual electricity use of ~207 Australian households (CSIRO, 2018).
  • Global data centre energy share – Warehouse-scale data centres account for roughly 1–2% of global electricity usage (Li et al., 2023).
  • Example of widespread use impact – If 10% of working Americans wrote a single 100-word email with ChatGPT weekly for a year, it would consume 121,517 MWh of electricity—equal to all household electricity use in Washington D.C. for 20 days—and 435,235,476 litres of water—equivalent to Rhode Island’s household water use over 1.5 days (Washington Post, 2024).
  • Energy use per AI task – Across different generative AI models, 1,000 inferences require between 0.047 kWh for text generation and 2.97 kWh for image generation (Luccioni et al., 2024).
  • Emissions per AI query – A single ChatGPT query produces an estimated 1.84 g CO₂, accounting for both query processing and model retraining (Tomlinson et al., 2023).

Annotations for Students (so they understand the why of the prompt

  1. Role setting (“Role play as an academic colleague…”) – This helps give the AI context about the audience, which affects the tone and complexity of its response
  2. Numbered structure – Breaks down the task into logical parts so the AI follows your intended flow.
  3. Cultural grounding instruction – Prevents generic storytelling and keeps it authentic.
  4. Evidence integration – By pasting your curated bullet points directly with references, you reduce hallucinations and keep the case study factually anchored.
  5. Framing the dilemma as values-based – Ensures discussion potential rather than just listing pros/cons.
  6. Clear output format – Tells the AI exactly how to structure the response for easy reading and teaching.
  7. Citation style requirement – Maintains academic integrity and consistency.
  8. AI acknowledgment - maintains transparency and by getting the AI to do this, ensures accuracy. 

What I Did After the AI Created the Case Study

  1. Fact-checked all references: Verified that every citation was real, matched the correct authors/year, and linked to the right source. Checked that the numbers (e.g., water usage, CO₂ emissions) matched the cited papers.
  2. Checked for accurate APA formatting: Ensured all in-text citations were correct and consistent. Confirmed that every source in the reference list appeared in the text (and vice versa).
  3. Removed or replaced hallucinated sources: Identified any references that didn’t exist or were misattributed. Replaced them with legitimate, relevant academic or credible sources.
  4. Verified factual accuracy beyond citations: Cross-checked real-world details like Pacific climate impacts, data centre energy use, and water scarcity statistics. Updated figures if more current or precise data was available.
  5. Edited for cultural appropriateness: Reviewed language for respectful representation of Pacific Island communities and Indigenous knowledge. Avoided stereotypes, overgeneralisation, or “parachute” descriptions.
  6. Strengthened narrative flow: Smoothed transitions between “Promise of AI” and “Concerns” so the shift didn’t feel abrupt. Adjusted the opening to anchor the reader in the Pacific Horizons story before introducing AI.
  7. Balanced tone: Ensured the benefits and risks were both represented fairly to avoid advocacy or fearmongering bias.
  8. Added nuance to complex issues: Expanded on concepts like Indigenous data sovereignty and labour exploitation to avoid oversimplification. Added quotes and voices from fictional characters to humanise the ethical concerns.
  9. Refined reflection questions: Made sure each question invited discussion rather than leading to a “right” answer. Ensured questions linked directly to the dilemmas in the case study.
  10. Final readability check: Ensured it worked for a diverse audience, from first-year students to experienced professionals. Shortened sentences where needed for clarity and accessibility.
