Projects

Ideas are cheap.

Projects rarely live or die by the brilliance of their ideas; they rise or fall on organisation. A clever concept can spark excitement, but without clear roles, steady coordination, realistic timelines, and disciplined follow-through, it drifts into confusion and stalls.

Working on a project (especially in a team) is a fine balance between enthusiastic ideas and slow-but-steady organisation. The resources below are intended to help you with ideas for your project, but more importantly, give you guidance on how to ensure you make progress completing it.

Strong projects are not defined by how original the idea is, but by how clearly the problem is understood, how well the solution is designed, how realistically trade-offs are considered, and how thoughtfully AI is used.

You may use one of these project scenarios as a starting point, adapt one, combine elements from more than one, or develop your own idea. These examples are here to help you think about scope, stakeholders, feasibility, ethics, and what meaningful project work can look like in practice.

  About This Project

This is a 40-hour interdisciplinary group project. You are not expected to solve a complex problem completely. You are expected to show thoughtful problem analysis, a realistic approach, responsible AI use, and a clear plan for how your project will work.

Use this page alongside the project overview, workshop materials, and your portfolio checkpoint instructions. The project scenarios below are starting points — not the only possible directions. They are designed to show the kinds of real-world problems, systems thinking, and responsible AI use this project is aiming for.

Using This Page For Your Portfolio Checkpoint

The Group Project Update Portfolio Checkpoint asks you to explain your project plan, group roles, intended AI use, and any risks or ethical issues you have identified. Use the project scenarios and approach guide on this page to help shape those sections.

As you work, keep track of details you may want to include in your portfolio, such as your project description, scope boundaries, stakeholders, team roles, AI tools and purposes, and ethical or practical risks.

Working in Teams

Team Formation

Your group of X–X students will work together across weeks 5–9. 

Once your group is confirmed:

  • Agree on a primary communication channel (e.g., Teams, Discord, WhatsApp) and a shared document space
  • Meet within the first 48 hours — even briefly — to review the brief together and share initial thoughts
  • Treat the mix of disciplines and backgrounds in your group as a strength. Interdisciplinary teams that draw on different expertise consistently produce more creative and robust outcomes than homogenous ones (Stokols et al., 2008)
  • Share a few sentences about your skills and interests so you can allocate roles thoughtfully from the start

Team Dynamics

Effective teamwork is built deliberately, not assumed. Most groups move through predictable stages — forming, storming, norming, and performing (Tuckman, 1965). Expect some friction early on; it is a sign the team is engaging seriously, not failing.

The single strongest predictor of team effectiveness is psychological safety — the shared belief that it is safe to speak up, take risks, and admit mistakes without fear of embarrassment or punishment (Edmondson, 1999). Build this early by making space for every voice.

Suggested roles (can overlap and rotate week to week):

  • Facilitator — runs meetings and keeps discussion focused
  • Notetaker — records decisions and action items
  • Coordinator — tracks task progress and deadlines
  • Researcher/s — gathers and synthesises information
  • Presenter — leads external communications and deliverables

When disagreements arise, treat them as a design problem: name the underlying concern, look for shared values, and generate options before deciding. Avoiding conflict early tends to make it worse later (Belbin, 2010).

Managing a Project

Managing Timelines

How teams structure their time is as important as how much time they have. Research on meeting science shows that well-structured, purposeful meetings significantly outperform unstructured ones for team coordination and accountability (Rogelberg, 2019). Two meeting types work well for short projects like this one:

  • Weekly check-in (15–20 mins) — Each person answers three questions: What did I complete since last time? What will I do before the next meeting? Is anything blocking my progress?
  • Working session (60–90 mins) — Collaborative work, review of drafts, and key decisions. These should be scheduled around workshop sessions, not in addition to them

For every meeting, no matter how short:

  1. Share a short agenda at least 24 hours beforehand
  2. Assign a facilitator and a notetaker at the start
  3. Start and end on time — respect everyone’s schedule
  4. Close with a list of action items, each with a named owner and a specific due date

A meeting without action items is a conversation. A meeting with action items is progress.

Planning

Scope creep — where a project grows beyond what is realistic to complete in the time available — is one of the most common reasons student projects stall. The solution is to define a minimum viable deliverable first: the simplest version that still addresses your core problem meaningfully. Additional features or depth can be layered in only once the core is working (Schwaber & Sutherland, 2020).

Design your project to be extensible: structure it so that each stage produces something real and evaluable, not just preparation for the next stage. This way, even if time runs short in the final week, you have a complete (if smaller) piece of work to present, rather than an unfinished large one.

Suggested milestones for this project:

  • Week 5 — Problem defined, team roles agreed, initial research plan documented
  • Week 6 — Approach designed, AI use cases identified and justified
  • Week 7 — First working draft or prototype complete and internally reviewed
  • Week 8 — Peer review, iteration, and ethical considerations addressed
  • Week 9 — Final output, portfolio reflection, and presentation ready
