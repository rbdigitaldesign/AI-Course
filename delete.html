Creating your AI Toolkit

This page is designed to help you explore a range of AI tools that can support your learning and research. You’ll try out different platforms, experiment with sample prompts, and reflect on how these tools can be used ethically and effectively in your academic work. The key here is to experiment with lots of different tools and prompts to see what works and what doesn't. We will aim to introduce you to tools you may be less familiar with, rather than sticking with the more common tools such as ChatGPT or Grammarly.

Prompt Engineering

Before jumping in and starting these tasks, it is critical to understand the importance of prompt engineering. AI doesn’t “know” what you want unless you tell it clearly (and even then, it isn't guaranteed). The way you phrase your prompt can dramatically change the quality, relevance, and usefulness of the response. Think of it like asking a lecturer for help: vague questions get vague answers, but specific, well-structured questions get exactly what you need.

We recommend this process (Lee & Palmer, 2025) for structuring your prompts. 

Lee, D., & Palmer, E. (2025). Prompt engineering in higher education: a systematic review to help inform curricula. International Journal of Educational Technology in Higher Education. 22(7) https://doi.org/10.1186/s41239-025-00503-7 

 

  1. Define role: Explain to the AI agent who it is.
  2. Provide background: Inform AI agent of context.
  3. Define objectives: Explain to the AI agent your goals.
  4. Set parameters: Allow or limit tolerances.
  5. Be precise: Ensure your instruction or question is concise and explicit.
  6. Specify format: Describe or even provide a template.
  7. Rinse and repeat: Critically assess the response in relation to your requirements and adjust your prompt accordingly to elicit more usable responses.

For example, my initial prompts might be “Summarise recent peer-reviewed research on climate change adaptation strategies in urban planning. Include key findings, methodologies, and gaps in the literature.”

But using the prompts structuring approach, we would add more specific detail and context to make it:

[to be colour coded or somehow annotated more clearly/presented nicely]

[1 · Define role] You are a senior research assistant specialising in climate adaptation and urban planning.

[2 · Provide background] Your audience is a cross-disciplinary city policy team (planners, engineers, public health). They need an evidence-based brief on climate change adaptation strategies deployable in urban contexts worldwide.

[3 · Define objectives] Summarise recent peer-reviewed research on climate change adaptation strategies in urban planning, covering (a) key findings, (b) methodologies, and (c) gaps in the literature. Extract practice-relevant insights.

[4 · Set parameters]

  • “Recent” = publications from 2019–present.

  • Use peer-reviewed journals and systematic reviews only; exclude editorials/opinion.

  • Prioritise studies with quantitative outcomes (e.g., heat-mortality reduction, flood-damage avoided).

  • Geographic scope: global, but note regional differences.

  • Length target: 800–1,000 words.

  • List 5–10 representative citations with DOI or journal name + year (no paywalled links required).

  • Note uncertainties/limitations explicitly.

[5 · Be precise] Treat “urban planning adaptation” as interventions such as heat mitigation (e.g., cool roofs, urban greening), flood/stormwater management (e.g., sponge city, blue-green infrastructure), land-use/zoning, building codes/retrofits, transportation/urban design for resilience, nature-based solutions, and governance/finance mechanisms that enable them. When reporting findings, include effect sizes or quantitative indicators whenever available.

[6 · Specify format]

Produce the output in the following structure:

  1. Executive Summary (≤150 words)

  2. Key Findings — bullet points grouped by intervention type

  3. Methodologies — brief comparison (RCTs, quasi-experimental, modelling, meta-analysis, case studies)

  4. Evidence Table — Markdown table with columns: Intervention | City/Region | Study Design | Metric/Effect | Equity/Co-benefits | Citation (Year)

  5. Gaps & Future Research — bullets

  6. References — 5–10 items, author(s), year, journal (no URLs needed)

[5 · Be precise — single instruction] If evidence is mixed or context-dependent, state where and why (climate zone, baseline infrastructure, socio-economic factors).

[3/4 · Objective + parameter for synthesis quality] Where multiple studies conflict, briefly reconcile by noting the strongest study designs and sample sizes.

[7] Rinse & Repeat cue: Run the prompt and critically evaluate the result, tweaking it as necessary.

Tool 1: Gemini (Google) – Deep Research Assistant

Purpose: Gemini is perhaps one of the most effective tools to support literature reviews, background research, and source comparison. It can help you quickly understand the current state of knowledge on a topic, identify key debates, and locate gaps in the research, all essential skills for university-level inquiry.

Try this: 

Choose a topic from one of your current or past courses—something you’re curious about or working on.

Use Gemini to:

  • Identify key studies and authors in the field.
  • Compare methodologies used across different papers.
  • Spot gaps or limitations in the existing research

Then ask yourself:

  • Are the sources Gemini provides credible and relevant?
  • Does it cite actual papers, or does it generalise?
  • How does this compare to what you’d find using Google Scholar or your university library database?

Sample Prompt*: “Summarise recent peer-reviewed research on [insert topic]. Include key findings, methodologies, and gaps in the literature.” -- for this prompt, in Gemini, you will need to click the settings toggle and turn on 'Deep Research'

*Note: for each of the activities on this page, we will provide you with a basic prompt; you should use the guidance above to improve it through prompt engineering before using it.

Tool 2: Perplexity – Research and Source Discovery

Purpose: Perplexity is a powerful AI search tool that blends conversational AI with real-time web access. It’s especially useful for finding sources, exploring unfamiliar topics, and comparing viewpoints. Unlike traditional search engines, Perplexity can provide concise summaries and cite its sources directly, making it a valuable tool for academic research.

Try this:
Choose a topic you’re considering for an assignment or presentation.

Use Perplexity to:

  • Explore definitions and key concepts.
  • Identify contrasting perspectives or debates.
  • Locate sources and check how they’re cited.

Then ask yourself:

  • How accurate are the definitions and key concept explanations?
  • Does Perplexity cite real articles or just summarise general knowledge?
  • How diverse are the contrasting viewpoints? Is this a deep analysis or just surface level?

Sample Prompt: “What are the main ethical concerns in using AI for student assessment in higher education? Provide definitions of key concepts and contrasting viewpoints.”

Tool 3: Podcast.ai or Podwise – Audio Summarisation

Purpose: These tools are designed to help you summarise long-form audio content, such as lectures, podcasts, or interviews. They’re especially useful for reviewing material, extracting key points, and making audio content more accessible.

Try this:
Choose a podcast or lecture relevant to your course.

Use Podwise to:

  • Generate a summary of the episode.
  • Identify key themes and recommendations.
  • Extract quotes or references for further research.

Then ask yourself:

  • Was the summary accurate and useful?
  • Did it miss any important points?
  • How could you use this tool to support your study habits?

Sample Prompt: “Summarise the key points from the [insert link and name of the source]. Highlight ethical guidelines and practical recommendations.”
